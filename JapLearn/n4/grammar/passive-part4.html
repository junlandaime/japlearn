Common Mistakes

Incorrect Formation of Passive

Incorrect:

食べるられる (taberu rareru)

Correct:

食べられる (taberareru)

For Group 2 verbs (ichidan verbs), you must remove the final る before adding られる. Don't simply add られる to the dictionary form.

Confusing Passive with Potential Form

Incorrect Interpretation:

この本は読まれる。

Kono hon wa yomareru.

(Interpreting as "This book can be read.")

Correct Interpretation:

この本は読まれる。

Kono hon wa yomareru.

This book is read. / This book is being read.

(Passive meaning)

The passive form and potential form can look identical for Group 1 verbs, which can cause confusion. The context will help you determine which meaning is intended.

Tip: If you want to clearly express the potential meaning, you can use ことができる after the dictionary form of the verb.

Using the Wrong Particle

Incorrect:

私は先生を褒められました。

Watashi wa sensei o homeraremashita.

Correct:

私は先生に褒められました。

Watashi wa sensei ni homeraremashita.

I was praised by the teacher.

In passive sentences, the agent (doer of the action) is marked with the particle に, not を. The particle を is used to mark the direct object in active sentences.

Incorrect Structure for Suffering Passive

Incorrect:

私の財布は盗まれました。

Watashi no saifu wa nusumaremashita.

(When trying to express "I had my wallet stolen.")

Correct:

私は財布を盗まれました。

Watashi wa saifu o nusumaremashita.

I had my wallet stolen.

In suffering passive sentences, the affected person is the subject (marked with は/が), and the thing that was affected is marked with を. The first example is a direct passive meaning "My wallet was stolen," which is different from the suffering passive "I had my wallet stolen."

Using Passive with Intransitive Verbs Incorrectly

While intransitive verbs can be used in the indirect passive, they cannot be used in the direct passive because they don't take a direct object.

Incorrect Usage:

雨は降られました。

Ame wa furaremashita.

(Trying to say "The rain was fallen.")

Correct Usage:

私は雨に降られました。

Watashi wa ame ni furaremashita.

I was rained on. (Indirect passive)

Intransitive verbs like 降る (furu, to fall), 来る (kuru, to come), and 死ぬ (shinu, to die) can only be used in the indirect passive, where the subject is affected by the action but is not the direct object of the action.

Summary

In this lesson, we've learned about the passive form in Japanese:

  • Formation:
    • Group 1 (Godan) verbs: う → あ + れる
    • Group 2 (Ichidan) verbs: る → られる
    • Irregular verbs: する → される, 来る → 来られる
  • Types of Passive:
    • Direct Passive: Similar to English passive voice
    • Indirect Passive: Subject is affected by an action indirectly
    • Suffering Passive: Subject is negatively affected by an action
  • Sentence Structure:
    • Direct Passive: Subject (receiver) + が/は + Agent (doer) + に + Verb (passive)
    • Indirect Passive: Subject (affected) + が/は + Agent + に + Verb (passive)
    • Suffering Passive: Subject (affected) + が/は + Object + を + Verb (passive)
  • Common Uses:
    • Describing experiences
    • Expressing inconvenience or annoyance
    • Describing things happening to possessions
    • Formal writing and news

The passive form is an important grammatical structure in Japanese that allows you to express a variety of nuances and perspectives. Understanding the different types of passive and when to use each one will greatly enhance your ability to communicate in Japanese.
