Polite Prohibition Forms

In most everyday situations, it's appropriate to use polite forms of prohibition. These forms are suitable for a wide range of contexts and are less likely to cause offense.

〜ないでください Form

The 〜ないでください form is the standard polite way to express prohibitions in Japanese. It's formed by using the negative stem of the verb (ない form) followed by で and ください.

Verb (ない form) + で + ください

ここで食べないでください。

Koko de tabenaide kudasai.

Please don't eat here.

携帯電話を使わないでください。

Keitai denwa o tsukawanaide kudasai.

Please don't use mobile phones.

ドアを閉めないでください。

Doa o shimenaide kudasai.

Please don't close the door.

遅れないでください。

Okurenaide kudasai.

Please don't be late.

This form is appropriate in most situations and is the safest choice when you're unsure which level of politeness to use. It's commonly used:

  • On public signs and notices
  • In instructions or manuals
  • When speaking to strangers
  • In customer service situations
  • In formal or business settings

〜ではいけません / 〜じゃいけません Form

The 〜ではいけません form is a formal way to express that something is not allowed or prohibited. It literally means "it won't do to..." or "it's not acceptable to..." and is often used in rules and regulations.

Verb (dictionary form) + ては/では + いけません

授業中に携帯電話を使ってはいけません。

Jugyouchuu ni keitai denwa o tsukatte wa ikemasen.

You must not use mobile phones during class.

ここでタバコを吸ってはいけません。

Koko de tabako o sutte wa ikemasen.

Smoking is not allowed here.

試験中に話してはいけません。

Shiken-chuu ni hanashite wa ikemasen.

You must not talk during the exam.

この部屋に入ってはいけません。

Kono heya ni haitte wa ikemasen.

You must not enter this room.

There are several variations of this form with slightly different nuances:

Form Formality Example
〜てはいけません Formal 走ってはいけません。
You must not run.
〜てはいけない Less formal 走ってはいけない。
You shouldn't run.
〜ちゃいけません Casual-polite 走っちゃいけません。
You mustn't run.
〜ちゃいけない Casual 走っちゃいけない。
You shouldn't run.

〜ないほうがいいです Form

The 〜ないほうがいいです form is used to give advice against doing something. It's a softer way to express prohibition, suggesting that it would be better not to do something rather than directly forbidding it.

Verb (ない form) + ほうが + いいです

その水は飲まないほうがいいです。

Sono mizu wa nomanai hou ga ii desu.

It's better not to drink that water.

明日は出かけないほうがいいですよ。

Ashita wa dekakenai hou ga ii desu yo.

It would be better not to go out tomorrow.

そんなに遅くまで起きていないほうがいいです。

Sonna ni osoku made okite inai hou ga ii desu.

It's better not to stay up so late.

熱があるなら、無理をしないほうがいいですよ。

Netsu ga aru nara, muri o shinai hou ga ii desu yo.

If you have a fever, it's better not to push yourself.

This form is particularly useful when:

  • Giving advice or suggestions
  • Warning someone about potential negative consequences
  • Making a prohibition sound less direct or harsh
  • Expressing concern for someone's well-being

Cultural Note: This indirect form of prohibition is very common in Japanese, as it allows the speaker to express a prohibition while maintaining harmony and avoiding direct confrontation. It's often preferred in situations where preserving the relationship is important.

Casual Prohibition Forms

In casual situations, such as among friends and family, less formal prohibition forms are used. These forms are more direct but appropriate in the right context.

〜ないで Form

The 〜ないで form is a casual way to tell someone not to do something. It's simply the negative te-form of the verb without the polite ください.

Verb (ない form) + で

そんなに心配しないで。

Sonna ni shinpai shinaide.

Don't worry so much.

泣かないで。

Nakanaide.

Don't cry.

ここに置かないで。

Koko ni okanaide.

Don't put it here.

遅れないで。

Okurenaide.

Don't be late.

This form is commonly used:

  • Among friends
  • Between family members
  • In casual situations
  • When making simple, everyday prohibitions

Tip: Adding ね (ne) at the end can soften this form: 「泣かないでね」 (Nakanaide ne - "Don't cry, okay?")

〜てはだめ / 〜ちゃだめ Form

The 〜てはだめ form expresses that something is "no good" or "not okay" to do. It's a relatively casual way to express prohibition.

Verb (て-form) + は + だめ

ここで遊んではだめです。

Koko de asonde wa dame desu.

You shouldn't play here. / It's not good to play here.

そんなことを言ってはだめよ。

Sonna koto o itte wa dame yo.

You shouldn't say such things.

授業中に寝ちゃだめだよ。

Jugyouchuu ni necha dame da yo.

You shouldn't sleep during class.

それを触っちゃだめ！

Sore o sawoccha dame!

Don't touch that!

This form has several variations with different levels of formality:

Form Formality Example
〜てはだめです Polite 走ってはだめです。
You shouldn't run.
〜てはだめだ Plain 走ってはだめだ。
You shouldn't run.
〜ちゃだめです Casual-polite 走っちゃだめです。
You shouldn't run.
〜ちゃだめ Very casual 走っちゃだめ。
Don't run.

〜な Form (Plain Prohibition)

The 〜な form is the plain prohibition form in Japanese. It's very direct and strong, and should be used with caution as it can sound rude in many contexts.

Verb (dictionary form) + な

動くな！

Ugoku na!

Don't move! (strong command)

そこに行くな！

Soko ni iku na!

Don't go there! (strong warning)

うるさい！話すな！

Urusai! Hanasu na!

Shut up! Don't talk! (very harsh)

心配するな。

Shinpai suru na.

Don't worry. (between close friends)

Warning: The 〜な form is very strong and can sound rude or aggressive. It's appropriate only in specific contexts:

  • Emergency situations
  • Military or police commands
  • Between very close male friends (in some cases)
  • In fiction (manga, anime, etc.)
  • When expressing strong emotions

In everyday situations, it's better to use more polite forms of prohibition.
