Learn how to give commands and make requests in Japanese

Introduction to the Imperative Form

The imperative form in Japanese is used to give commands, make requests, or issue instructions. Unlike English, which primarily uses one form for commands ("Go!", "Stop!", etc.), Japanese has several different ways to express commands, each with varying levels of politeness and formality.

窓を開けろ！

Mado o akero!

Open the window! (Strong command)

窓を開けてください。

Mado o akete kudasai.

Please open the window. (Polite request)

Understanding the different imperative forms and when to use them is crucial for effective communication in Japanese. Using the wrong form can make you sound rude or overly formal, depending on the situation.

Types of Imperative Forms

  • • Plain Imperative: Direct commands (casual or harsh)
  • • 〜てください Form: Polite requests
  • • 〜なさい Form: Used by superiors to subordinates
  • • 〜て Form: Casual requests among friends
  • • 〜てくれる？ Form: Asking for favors
  • • 〜ていただけませんか Form: Very polite requests

When to Use Each Form

  • • Plain Imperative: Emergency situations, military commands, manga/anime
  • • 〜てください: Standard polite requests in most situations
  • • 〜なさい: Parents to children, teachers to students
  • • 〜て Form: Among close friends and family
  • • 〜てくれる？: Casual requests with friends
  • • 〜ていただけませんか: Formal situations, with superiors

Important Warning

The plain imperative form (〜ろ/〜え) is considered very harsh and can be offensive if used incorrectly. It's generally only appropriate in specific contexts like emergencies, military settings, or when depicted in manga/anime. In everyday conversation, it's better to use more polite forms unless you're very familiar with Japanese culture and language nuances.

Plain Imperative Form

The plain imperative form is the most direct and forceful way to give commands in Japanese. It's considered very strong and can sound rude in many contexts, so it should be used with caution.

How to Form the Plain Imperative

The formation of the plain imperative depends on the type of verb:

Group 1: U-Verbs (五段動詞)

For u-verbs, replace the final ~u with ~e.

Dictionary form: 最後の「う」→「え」

Dictionary Form Imperative Form Example
話す (はなす)
to speak		 話せ (はなせ) 日本語で話せ！
Speak in Japanese!
書く (かく)
to write		 書け (かけ) ここに名前を書け！
Write your name here!
待つ (まつ)
to wait		 待て (まて) そこで待て！
Wait there!
読む (よむ)
to read		 読め (よめ) この本を読め！
Read this book!
飲む (のむ)
to drink		 飲め (のめ) この薬を飲め！
Drink this medicine!

Group 2: RU-Verbs (一段動詞)

For ru-verbs, remove the final ~ru and add ~ろ.

Dictionary form: 「る」を取って、「ろ」を付ける

Dictionary Form Imperative Form Example
食べる (たべる)
to eat		 食べろ (たべろ) 早く食べろ！
Eat quickly!
見る (みる)
to see/look		 見ろ (みろ) あそこを見ろ！
Look over there!
起きる (おきる)
to wake up		 起きろ (おきろ) 早く起きろ！
Wake up early!
出る (でる)
to go out		 出ろ (でろ) ここから出ろ！
Get out of here!

Group 3: Irregular Verbs (不規則動詞)

The two irregular verbs have their own imperative forms:

Dictionary Form Imperative Form Example
する
to do		 しろ 早くしろ！
Hurry up! (lit. Do quickly!)
来る (くる)
to come		 来い (こい) ここに来い！
Come here!

Note: The plain imperative form is rarely used in everyday conversation. It's mainly found in emergency situations, military commands, or in fiction like manga and anime. Using it in normal conversation can sound extremely rude or aggressive.

Appropriate Contexts for Plain Imperative

While the plain imperative form is generally avoided in everyday conversation, there are specific contexts where it is appropriate or commonly used:

1. Emergency Situations

逃げろ！

Nigero!

Run away! (in a dangerous situation)

助けて！

Tasukete!

Help! (This is actually the te-form used as an imperative)

2. Military or Police Commands

止まれ！

Tomare!

Stop! (as might be shouted by police)

気をつけ！

Ki o tsuke!

Attention! (military command)

3. In Fiction (Manga, Anime, etc.)

黙れ！

Damare!

Shut up! (often used by angry characters)

死ね！

Shine!

Die! (extremely harsh, only in fiction)

4. Set Phrases and Expressions

頑張れ！

Ganbare!

Do your best! (cheering at sports events)

気を付けろ！

Ki o tsukero!

Be careful! (warning of danger)

Polite Request Forms

In contrast to the plain imperative, Japanese has several polite forms for making requests or giving instructions. These forms are much more commonly used in everyday conversation.

〜てください Form

The 〜てください form is the standard polite way to make requests in Japanese. It's formed by using the te-form of the verb followed by ください (kudasai).

Verb (て-form) + ください

ドアを開けてください。

Doa o akete kudasai.

Please open the door.

ここに名前を書いてください。

Koko ni namae o kaite kudasai.

Please write your name here.

静かにしてください。

Shizuka ni shite kudasai.

Please be quiet.

明日の朝9時に来てください。

Ashita no asa ku-ji ni kite kudasai.

Please come at 9 AM tomorrow morning.

This form is appropriate in most situations and is the safest choice when you're unsure which level of politeness to use. It's commonly used:

  • When speaking to strangers
  • In customer service situations
  • When making requests to people you don't know well
  • In formal or business settings

〜なさい Form

The 〜なさい form is used by people in positions of authority when giving instructions to those of lower status, particularly by parents to children or teachers to students. It's formed by adding なさい (nasai) to the verb stem (masu-form without ます).

Verb stem (ます-form without ます) + なさい

早く食べなさい。

Hayaku tabenasai.

Eat quickly. (parent to child)

宿題をしなさい。

Shukudai o shinasai.

Do your homework. (teacher to student)

手を洗いなさい。

Te o arainasai.

Wash your hands. (parent to child)

ここに座りなさい。

Koko ni suwarinasai.

Sit here. (teacher to student)

Note: While 〜なさい is more polite than the plain imperative, it still implies authority and should not be used with people of equal or higher status. It's often used with a softer tone of voice than the plain imperative.

〜て Form as a Request

The simple て-form of a verb can be used as a casual request among friends and family. It's less formal than 〜てください but not as harsh as the plain imperative.

Verb (て-form)

ちょっと待って。

Chotto matte.

Wait a moment. (casual)

これ見て。

Kore mite.

Look at this. (casual)

早く来て。

Hayaku kite.

Come quickly. (casual)

手伝って。

Tetsudatte.

Help me. (casual)

This form is commonly used:

  • Among friends
  • Between family members
  • In casual situations
  • When making simple, everyday requests

Tip: Adding ちょっと (chotto, "a little") or ね (ne) at the end can soften the request: 「ちょっと待ってね」 (Chotto matte ne - "Wait a moment, okay?")

More Polite Request Forms

Japanese offers several more polite forms for making requests, especially when asking for favors or speaking to superiors. These forms allow you to express varying degrees of politeness depending on the social context.

〜てくれる？ Form

The 〜てくれる？ form is used to ask someone to do something for you in a casual way. It literally means "Will you do ~ for me?" and is formed by combining the te-form with the helping verb くれる (kureru) in its question form.

Verb (て-form) + くれる？

窓を開けてくれる？

Mado o akete kureru?

Can you open the window for me?

明日手伝ってくれる？

Ashita tetsudatte kureru?

Can you help me tomorrow?

これを持ってくれる？

Kore o motte kureru?

Can you hold this for me?

電話番号を教えてくれる？

Denwa bangou o oshiete kureru?

Can you tell me your phone number?

This form can be made more polite by using the formal form of くれる:

窓を開けてくれますか？

Mado o akete kuremasu ka?

Would you open the window for me? (more polite)

明日手伝ってくれませんか？

Ashita tetsudatte kuremasen ka?

Would you help me tomorrow? (more polite)

〜ていただけませんか Form

The 〜ていただけませんか form is one of the most polite ways to make a request in Japanese. It's formed by combining the te-form with the humble verb いただく (itadaku) in its potential negative question form.

Verb (て-form) + いただけませんか

書類に署名していただけませんか。

Shorui ni shomei shite itadakemasen ka.

Would you be so kind as to sign this document?

明日までに完成していただけませんか。

Ashita made ni kansei shite itadakemasen ka.

Would it be possible for you to complete this by tomorrow?

少々お待ちいただけませんか。

Shoushou omachi itadakemasen ka.

Would you mind waiting for a moment?

ご連絡先を教えていただけませんか。

Go-renrakusaki o oshiete itadakemasen ka.

Would you be kind enough to give me your contact information?

Usage Note: This form is appropriate in formal situations, such as business settings, when speaking to superiors, or when making requests to strangers in a professional context. It shows a high level of respect and humility.

Other Polite Request Forms

Japanese has several other expressions that can be used to make polite requests:

〜てもらえますか / 〜てもらえませんか

Similar to 〜てくれますか but slightly more polite

この書類をコピーしてもらえますか。

Kono shorui o kopii shite moraemasu ka.

Could you make a copy of this document for me?

明日までに報告書を提出してもらえませんか。

Ashita made ni houkokusho o teishutsu shite moraemasen ka.

Could you submit the report by tomorrow?

〜ていただきたいです

Expresses a polite wish for someone to do something

明日の会議に参加していただきたいです。

Ashita no kaigi ni sanka shite itadakitai desu.

I would like you to participate in tomorrow's meeting.

この資料に目を通していただきたいです。

Kono shiryou ni me o tooshite itadakitai desu.

I would like you to look through these materials.

〜てくださいませんか

Very polite form using the honorific form of くれる

もう一度説明してくださいませんか。

Mou ichido setsumei shite kudasaimasen ka.

Would you please explain it once more?

お名前を教えてくださいませんか。

Onamae o oshiete kudasaimasen ka.

Would you please tell me your name?

Special Usages and Considerations

There are several special considerations and usages related to the imperative form in Japanese that are important to understand.

Softening Commands

Japanese has several ways to soften commands or requests to make them sound less direct or harsh:

1. Adding ね (ne)

ちょっと待ってね。

Chotto matte ne.

Wait a moment, okay?

Adding ね at the end makes the request sound more friendly and less like a command.

2. Using ちょっと (chotto)

ちょっと手伝ってくれませんか。

Chotto tetsudatte kuremasen ka.

Could you help me a little?

Adding ちょっと ("a little") before a request can make it sound less demanding.

3. Using Conditional Forms

よかったら、これを食べてみてください。

Yokattara, kore o tabete mite kudasai.

If you'd like, please try eating this.

Using conditional expressions like よかったら ("if it's okay") or もしよければ ("if you don't mind") can soften requests.

4. Adding 〜てみる (te miru)

この問題を解いてみてください。

Kono mondai o toite mite kudasai.

Please try solving this problem.

Adding 〜てみる ("to try doing") can make a command sound more like a suggestion.

Commands in Negative Form (Prohibitions)

To tell someone not to do something (prohibitions), different patterns are used:

1. 〜ないでください

ここで写真を撮らないでください。

Koko de shashin o toranaide kudasai.

Please don't take photos here.

This is the polite way to tell someone not to do something. It's formed with the negative stem of the verb + ないで + ください.

2. 〜ないで

泣かないで。

Nakanaide.

Don't cry. (casual)

This is the casual form used among friends and family.

3. 〜な

そこに行くな！

Soko ni iku na!

Don't go there! (strong prohibition)

This is the plain form of prohibition, formed by adding な to the dictionary form of the verb. It's very strong and should be used with caution.

4. 〜ではいけません / 〜ではだめです

授業中に携帯電話を使ってはいけません。

Jugyouchuu ni keitai denwa o tsukatte wa ikemasen.

You must not use mobile phones during class.

These expressions indicate that something is not allowed or prohibited.

Note: The prohibitive form 〜な is very strong and can sound rude. It's mainly used in emergencies, by people in authority, or in fiction. In everyday situations, it's better to use 〜ないでください or other polite forms.

Imperative in Written Instructions

In written instructions, such as recipes, manuals, or signs, specific forms of the imperative are commonly used:

1. 〜てください in Instructions

使用前によく振ってください。

Shiyou mae ni yoku futte kudasai.

Please shake well before use.

This polite form is common in product instructions and public signs.

2. Dictionary Form in Recipes

野菜を細かく切る。鍋に油を熱する。

Yasai o komakaku kiru. Nabe ni abura o nessuru.

Cut the vegetables finely. Heat oil in a pot.

In recipes, the plain dictionary form is often used for instructions, not as a command but as a neutral description of the process.

3. 〜こと in Official Instructions

入場前に手を消毒すること。

Nyuujou mae ni te o shoudoku suru koto.

Disinfect hands before entering.

Adding こと after the dictionary form is common in official instructions, rules, or signs.

4. 〜ないでください in Prohibitions

芝生に入らないでください。

Shibafu ni hairanaide kudasai.

Please do not step on the grass.

This form is commonly used on signs indicating prohibitions.

Exercise 1: Plain Imperative Form

Convert the following verbs to their plain imperative form.

1. 行く (いく) - to go

行け (いけ)

Ike - Go! (U-verb: change final う to え)

2. 食べる (たべる) - to eat

食べろ (たべろ)

Tabero - Eat! (RU-verb: remove る and add ろ)

3. 聞く (きく) - to listen/ask

聞け (きけ)

Kike - Listen! (U-verb: change final う to え)

4. 見る (みる) - to see/look

見ろ (みろ)

Miro - Look! (RU-verb: remove る and add ろ)

5. する - to do

しろ

Shiro - Do! (Irregular verb)

6. 来る (くる) - to come

来い (こい)

Koi - Come! (Irregular verb)

Exercise 2: Choosing the Right Form

Choose the most appropriate imperative form for each situation.

1. You want to ask a stranger to take your photo. Which form would you use?

写真を撮ってもらえませんか。 / 写真を撮ってくださいませんか。

Shashin o totte moraemasen ka. / Shashin o totte kudasaimasen ka.

When speaking to a stranger, a polite form like 〜てもらえませんか or 〜てくださいませんか is appropriate.

2. A mother telling her child to clean their room. Which form would be appropriate?

部屋を掃除しなさい。

Heya o souji shinasai.

The 〜なさい form is commonly used by parents when giving instructions to their children.

3. You're asking a close friend to wait for you. Which form would you use?

ちょっと待って。 / 待ってくれる？

Chotto matte. / Matte kureru?

With close friends, the casual て-form or 〜てくれる？ form is appropriate.

4. A police officer ordering someone to stop during an emergency. Which form would be used?

止まれ！

Tomare!

In emergency situations or by authorities giving commands, the plain imperative form is used.

5. You're asking your boss for time off. Which form would be most appropriate?

休みを取らせていただけませんか。

Yasumi o torasete itadakemasen ka.

When speaking to superiors, the very polite 〜ていただけませんか form is appropriate.

Exercise 3: Converting to Negative Commands

Convert the following positive commands to negative commands (prohibitions).

1. ここで食べてください。 → (Please don't eat here.)

ここで食べないでください。

Koko de tabenaide kudasai.

2. 入って。 → (Don't come in.)

入らないで。

Hairanaide.

3. 行け！ → (Don't go!)

行くな！

Iku na!

4. 部屋を掃除しなさい。 → (Don't clean the room.)

部屋を掃除しないでください。 / 部屋を掃除しなくていいです。

Heya o souji shinaide kudasai. / Heya o souji shinakute ii desu.

The second option means "You don't have to clean the room" which is often more natural than a direct prohibition in this context.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Using the Plain Imperative in Everyday Conversation

❌ 窓を開けろ！ (to a colleague or stranger)

Mado o akero!

✓ 窓を開けてもらえますか？

Mado o akete moraemasu ka?

The plain imperative form is too harsh for everyday conversation and can sound rude. Use polite request forms instead.

Mistake 2: Using 〜なさい with People of Equal or Higher Status

❌ 先生、ここに座りなさい。

Sensei, koko ni suwarinasai.

✓ 先生、こちらにお座りください。

Sensei, kochira ni osuwari kudasai.

The 〜なさい form is used by people in positions of authority. Using it with equals or superiors is inappropriate.

Mistake 3: Not Softening Requests Appropriately

❌ 手伝ってください。 (abrupt request to a superior)

Tetsudatte kudasai.

✓ 手伝っていただけませんか。

Tetsudatte itadakemasen ka.

When making requests to superiors or in formal situations, it's important to use appropriately polite forms.

Mistake 4: Using the Wrong Prohibition Form

❌ 行くな！ (to a customer or colleague)

Iku na!

✓ 行かないでください。

Ikanaide kudasai.

The 〜な prohibition form is very harsh. In most situations, 〜ないでください is more appropriate.

Summary

Key Takeaways

  • • Japanese has multiple imperative forms, each with different levels of politeness and appropriate contexts for use.
  • • The plain imperative (〜ろ/〜え) is very direct and should be used with caution, mainly in emergencies or fiction.
  • • The 〜てください form is the standard polite way to make requests in most situations.
  • • The 〜なさい form is used by people in authority when giving instructions to subordinates.
  • • The simple て-form can be used for casual requests among friends and family.
  • • More polite forms like 〜ていただけませんか are appropriate when speaking to superiors or in formal situations.
  • • Negative commands (prohibitions) have their own forms, with 〜ないでください being the standard polite form.
