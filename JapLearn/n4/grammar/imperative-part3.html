More Polite Request Forms

Japanese offers several more polite forms for making requests, especially when asking for favors or speaking to superiors. These forms allow you to express varying degrees of politeness depending on the social context.

〜てくれる？ Form

The 〜てくれる？ form is used to ask someone to do something for you in a casual way. It literally means "Will you do ~ for me?" and is formed by combining the te-form with the helping verb くれる (kureru) in its question form.

Verb (て-form) + くれる？

窓を開けてくれる？

Mado o akete kureru?

Can you open the window for me?

明日手伝ってくれる？

Ashita tetsudatte kureru?

Can you help me tomorrow?

これを持ってくれる？

Kore o motte kureru?

Can you hold this for me?

電話番号を教えてくれる？

Denwa bangou o oshiete kureru?

Can you tell me your phone number?

This form can be made more polite by using the formal form of くれる:

窓を開けてくれますか？

Mado o akete kuremasu ka?

Would you open the window for me? (more polite)

明日手伝ってくれませんか？

Ashita tetsudatte kuremasen ka?

Would you help me tomorrow? (more polite)

〜ていただけませんか Form

The 〜ていただけませんか form is one of the most polite ways to make a request in Japanese. It's formed by combining the te-form with the humble verb いただく (itadaku) in its potential negative question form.

Verb (て-form) + いただけませんか

書類に署名していただけませんか。

Shorui ni shomei shite itadakemasen ka.

Would you be so kind as to sign this document?

明日までに完成していただけませんか。

Ashita made ni kansei shite itadakemasen ka.

Would it be possible for you to complete this by tomorrow?

少々お待ちいただけませんか。

Shoushou omachi itadakemasen ka.

Would you mind waiting for a moment?

ご連絡先を教えていただけませんか。

Go-renrakusaki o oshiete itadakemasen ka.

Would you be kind enough to give me your contact information?

Usage Note: This form is appropriate in formal situations, such as business settings, when speaking to superiors, or when making requests to strangers in a professional context. It shows a high level of respect and humility.

Other Polite Request Forms

Japanese has several other expressions that can be used to make polite requests:

〜てもらえますか / 〜てもらえませんか

Similar to 〜てくれますか but slightly more polite

この書類をコピーしてもらえますか。

Kono shorui o kopii shite moraemasu ka.

Could you make a copy of this document for me?

明日までに報告書を提出してもらえませんか。

Ashita made ni houkokusho o teishutsu shite moraemasen ka.

Could you submit the report by tomorrow?

〜ていただきたいです

Expresses a polite wish for someone to do something

明日の会議に参加していただきたいです。

Ashita no kaigi ni sanka shite itadakitai desu.

I would like you to participate in tomorrow's meeting.

この資料に目を通していただきたいです。

Kono shiryou ni me o tooshite itadakitai desu.

I would like you to look through these materials.

〜てくださいませんか

Very polite form using the honorific form of くれる

もう一度説明してくださいませんか。

Mou ichido setsumei shite kudasaimasen ka.

Would you please explain it once more?

お名前を教えてくださいませんか。

Onamae o oshiete kudasaimasen ka.

Would you please tell me your name?

Special Usages and Considerations

There are several special considerations and usages related to the imperative form in Japanese that are important to understand.

Softening Commands

Japanese has several ways to soften commands or requests to make them sound less direct or harsh:

1. Adding ね (ne)

ちょっと待ってね。

Chotto matte ne.

Wait a moment, okay?

Adding ね at the end makes the request sound more friendly and less like a command.

2. Using ちょっと (chotto)

ちょっと手伝ってくれませんか。

Chotto tetsudatte kuremasen ka.

Could you help me a little?

Adding ちょっと ("a little") before a request can make it sound less demanding.

3. Using Conditional Forms

よかったら、これを食べてみてください。

Yokattara, kore o tabete mite kudasai.

If you'd like, please try eating this.

Using conditional expressions like よかったら ("if it's okay") or もしよければ ("if you don't mind") can soften requests.

4. Adding 〜てみる (te miru)

この問題を解いてみてください。

Kono mondai o toite mite kudasai.

Please try solving this problem.

Adding 〜てみる ("to try doing") can make a command sound more like a suggestion.

Commands in Negative Form (Prohibitions)

To tell someone not to do something (prohibitions), different patterns are used:

1. 〜ないでください

ここで写真を撮らないでください。

Koko de shashin o toranaide kudasai.

Please don't take photos here.

This is the polite way to tell someone not to do something. It's formed with the negative stem of the verb + ないで + ください.

2. 〜ないで

泣かないで。

Nakanaide.

Don't cry. (casual)

This is the casual form used among friends and family.

3. 〜な

そこに行くな！

Soko ni iku na!

Don't go there! (strong prohibition)

This is the plain form of prohibition, formed by adding な to the dictionary form of the verb. It's very strong and should be used with caution.

4. 〜ではいけません / 〜ではだめです

授業中に携帯電話を使ってはいけません。

Jugyouchuu ni keitai denwa o tsukatte wa ikemasen.

You must not use mobile phones during class.

These expressions indicate that something is not allowed or prohibited.

Note: The prohibitive form 〜な is very strong and can sound rude. It's mainly used in emergencies, by people in authority, or in fiction. In everyday situations, it's better to use 〜ないでください or other polite forms.

Imperative in Written Instructions

In written instructions, such as recipes, manuals, or signs, specific forms of the imperative are commonly used:

1. 〜てください in Instructions

使用前によく振ってください。

Shiyou mae ni yoku futte kudasai.

Please shake well before use.

This polite form is common in product instructions and public signs.

2. Dictionary Form in Recipes

野菜を細かく切る。鍋に油を熱する。

Yasai o komakaku kiru. Nabe ni abura o nessuru.

Cut the vegetables finely. Heat oil in a pot.

In recipes, the plain dictionary form is often used for instructions, not as a command but as a neutral description of the process.

3. 〜こと in Official Instructions

入場前に手を消毒すること。

Nyuujou mae ni te o shoudoku suru koto.

Disinfect hands before entering.

Adding こと after the dictionary form is common in official instructions, rules, or signs.

4. 〜ないでください in Prohibitions

芝生に入らないでください。

Shibafu ni hairanaide kudasai.

Please do not step on the grass.

This form is commonly used on signs indicating prohibitions.
