Japanese Questions

How to ask and answer questions in Japanese

Introduction to Japanese Questions

Asking questions is one of the most fundamental communication skills in any language. In Japanese, forming questions is relatively simple compared to English, as it doesn't require changing the word order or using auxiliary verbs like "do" or "does."

Instead, Japanese questions are typically formed by either adding the question particle か (ka) at the end of a sentence or using question words (like "what," "where," etc.). The intonation also rises at the end of a question, similar to many other languages.

Note: In Japanese, you can also ask a question simply by raising your intonation at the end of a statement, especially in casual conversation, but using the question particle か (ka) is more formal and clearer in writing.

Yes/No Questions

Yes/No questions in Japanese (also called closed questions) can be formed by simply adding the question particle か (ka) to the end of a statement. The word order remains the same as in a declarative sentence.

Basic Structure for Yes/No Questions

Statement + か (ka)

Examples:

  • Statement: 田中さんは学生です。 (Tanaka-san wa gakusei desu.) - Tanaka is a student.
  • Question: 田中さんは学生ですか。 (Tanaka-san wa gakusei desu ka?) - Is Tanaka a student?
  • Statement: マリーさんは日本語を話します。 (Marī-san wa nihongo o hanashimasu.) - Mary speaks Japanese.
  • Question: マリーさんは日本語を話しますか。 (Marī-san wa nihongo o hanashimasu ka?) - Does Mary speak Japanese?

Casual Yes/No Questions

In casual conversation, the question particle か (ka) is often dropped, and the question is indicated by rising intonation. In some cases, の (no) or かな (kana) may be used instead of か (ka).

Politeness Level Example Question Translation
Formal/Polite あなたは日本人ですか。
(Anata wa nihonjin desu ka?)		 Are you Japanese?
Casual with の (no) あなたは日本人なの？
(Anata wa nihonjin na no?)		 Are you Japanese?
Very casual (intonation) 日本人？
(Nihonjin?) (with rising intonation)		 You're Japanese?
Self-directed with かな (kana) 彼は日本人かな。
(Kare wa nihonjin kana.)		 I wonder if he's Japanese.

Responding to Yes/No Questions

Responding to Yes/No questions in Japanese is slightly different from English. Instead of saying "yes" or "no" directly, Japanese responses confirm or deny the truth of the statement.

Example Responses:

Question: 日本人ですか。 (Nihonjin desu ka?) - Are you Japanese?

  • Affirmative: はい、日本人です。 (Hai, nihonjin desu.) - Yes, I am Japanese.
  • Negative: いいえ、日本人ではありません。 (Iie, nihonjin dewa arimasen.) - No, I am not Japanese.

Question: 日本語を話しませんか。 (Nihongo o hanashimasen ka?) - Don't you speak Japanese?

  • Affirmative (agreeing with the negative): はい、話しません。 (Hai, hanashimasen.) - That's correct, I don't speak Japanese.
  • Negative (disagreeing with the negative): いいえ、話します。 (Iie, hanashimasu.) - No, I do speak Japanese.

This pattern can be confusing for English speakers, as in Japanese, "hai" (はい) means "that's correct" rather than simply "yes," and "iie" (いいえ) means "that's incorrect" rather than simply "no."

Wh-Questions (Information Questions)

Wh-questions (also called open questions) in Japanese use question words (interrogatives) to ask for specific information. Unlike English, Japanese question words stay in the same position where the answer would be in a statement.

Common Japanese Question Words

Japanese Romaji English Example
何 nani / nan what これは何ですか。
(Kore wa nan desu ka?)
What is this?
誰 dare who あの人は誰ですか。
(Ano hito wa dare desu ka?)
Who is that person?
どこ doko where トイレはどこですか。
(Toire wa doko desu ka?)
Where is the toilet?
いつ itsu when 試験はいつですか。
(Shiken wa itsu desu ka?)
When is the exam?
なぜ・どうして naze / dōshite why どうして日本語を勉強していますか。
(Dōshite nihongo o benkyō shite imasu ka?)
Why are you studying Japanese?
どう dō how 調子はどうですか。
(Chōshi wa dō desu ka?)
How are you?
どれ dore which one どれが好きですか。
(Dore ga suki desu ka?)
Which one do you like?
どの dono which どの本を読みましたか。
(Dono hon o yomimashita ka?)
Which book did you read?
いくら ikura how much (cost) このカバンはいくらですか。
(Kono kaban wa ikura desu ka?)
How much is this bag?
何個・いくつ nan-ko / ikutsu how many リンゴは何個ありますか。
(Ringo wa nan-ko arimasu ka?)
How many apples are there?

Asking "What" Questions

The Japanese word for "what" is 何 (nani), but it can change to なん (nan) depending on what follows it.

Examples of 何/なん (nani/nan) Usage:

  • これは何ですか。 (Kore wa nan desu ka?) - What is this?
  • あなたの名前は何ですか。 (Anata no namae wa nan desu ka?) - What is your name?
  • 何時ですか。 (Nanji desu ka?) - What time is it?
  • 何曜日ですか。 (Nanyōbi desu ka?) - What day of the week is it?
  • 何歳ですか。 (Nansai desu ka?) - How old are you?
  • 何人ですか。 (Nannin desu ka?) - How many people?

Responding to Wh-Questions

When responding to wh-questions, simply provide the requested information. You can use the same sentence pattern as the question but replace the question word with the answer.

Example Responses:

Question: トイレはどこですか。 (Toire wa doko desu ka?) - Where is the toilet?

Response: トイレは2階です。 (Toire wa ni-kai desu.) - The toilet is on the second floor.

Question: あなたの名前は何ですか。 (Anata no namae wa nan desu ka?) - What is your name?

Response: 私の名前は田中です。 (Watashi no namae wa Tanaka desu.) - My name is Tanaka.

Question: どうして日本語を勉強していますか。 (Dōshite nihongo o benkyō shite imasu ka?) - Why are you studying Japanese?

Response: 日本に行きたいからです。 (Nihon ni ikitai kara desu.) - Because I want to go to Japan.

Choice Questions

Choice questions in Japanese offer the listener a choice between two or more options. These are typically formed using the pattern "A か B" (A ka B) which means "A or B."

Basic Structure for Choice Questions

Option A + か (ka) + Option B + ですか (desu ka)

Examples:

  • コーヒーかお茶が飲みたいですか。
    (Kōhī ka ocha ga nomitai desu ka?)
    Would you like to drink coffee or tea?
  • 電車かバスで行きますか。
    (Densha ka basu de ikimasu ka?)
    Will you go by train or bus?

When responding to choice questions, you can simply state your preference.

Example Responses:

Question: コーヒーかお茶が飲みたいですか。
(Kōhī ka ocha ga nomitai desu ka?)
Would you like to drink coffee or tea?

Response: コーヒーをお願いします。 (Kōhī o onegaishimasu.) - Coffee, please.

Question: 電車かバスで行きますか。
(Densha ka basu de ikimasu ka?)
Will you go by train or bus?

Response: 電車で行きます。 (Densha de ikimasu.) - I'll go by train.

Offering More Options

You can offer more than two options by repeating the pattern.

Examples:

コーヒーか、お茶か、ジュースが飲みたいですか。

Kōhī ka, ocha ka, jūsu ga nomitai desu ka?

Would you like to drink coffee, tea, or juice?

Common Question Patterns

Japanese has several common question patterns that are used in everyday conversation.

Pattern Meaning Example
〜は？
(~wa?)		 What about...? 田中さんは？
(Tanaka-san wa?)
What about Tanaka?
〜ませんか？
(~masen ka?)		 Won't you...?
(Invitation)		 一緒に行きませんか？
(Issho ni ikimasen ka?)
Won't you go together with me?
〜ましょうか？
(~mashou ka?)		 Shall we...?
(Offering help)		 荷物を持ちましょうか？
(Nimotsu o mochimashou ka?)
Shall I carry your luggage?
〜でしょうか？
(~deshou ka?)		 I wonder...?
(Polite inquiry)		 明日は雨でしょうか？
(Ashita wa ame deshou ka?)
I wonder if it will rain tomorrow?
〜んですか？
(~n desu ka?)		 Is it that...?
(Seeking explanation)		 日本語を勉強しているんですか？
(Nihongo o benkyō shite iru n desu ka?)
Is it that you're studying Japanese?

Asking Questions Politely

In Japanese culture, politeness is important, especially when asking questions. Here are some ways to make your questions more polite.

Using すみません (sumimasen)

Start your question with すみません to apologize for the interruption or inconvenience.

Example:

すみません、トイレはどこですか。

Sumimasen, toire wa doko desu ka?

Excuse me, where is the toilet?

Using 〜ていただけませんか

This very polite pattern asks if someone would be willing to do something for you.

Example:

写真を撮っていただけませんか。

Shashin o totte itadakemasen ka?

Would you be kind enough to take a photo?

Using 〜でしょうか (deshou ka)

This adds a level of uncertainty that makes the question sound less direct and more polite.

Example:

お名前は何でしょうか。

Onamae wa nan deshou ka?

May I ask what your name is?

Using 〜ませんか (masen ka) for Invitations

This pattern is used for polite invitations or suggestions.

Example:

一緒にお昼ご飯を食べませんか。

Issho ni ohiru gohan o tabemasen ka?

Would you like to have lunch together?

Cultural Tip: In Japan, being direct can sometimes be considered impolite. Using these polite question forms, especially with people you don't know well or in formal situations, shows respect and good manners.

Practice Exercises

Test your understanding of Japanese questions with these exercises:

Exercise 1: Yes/No Questions

Convert the following statements into Yes/No questions by adding か (ka):

  1. あなたは学生です。 (Anata wa gakusei desu.) あなたは学生ですか。 (Anata wa gakusei desu ka?)
  2. 彼は日本人です。 (Kare wa nihonjin desu.) 彼は日本人ですか。 (Kare wa nihonjin desu ka?)
  3. あの店は高いです。 (Ano mise wa takai desu.) あの店は高いですか。 (Ano mise wa takai desu ka?)
  4. 明日雨が降ります。 (Ashita ame ga furimasu.) 明日雨が降りますか。 (Ashita ame ga furimasu ka?)

Exercise 2: Wh-Questions

Fill in the blanks with the appropriate question word:

  1. あなたの趣味は＿＿＿ですか。 (Anata no shumi wa ___ desu ka?) 何 (nani) - What is your hobby?
  2. ＿＿＿日本語を勉強していますか。 (___ nihongo o benkyō shite imasu ka?) どうして (dōshite) or なぜ (naze) - Why are you studying Japanese?
  3. あのかばんは＿＿＿ですか。 (Ano kaban wa ___ desu ka?) いくら (ikura) - How much is that bag?
  4. 授業は＿＿＿始まりますか。 (Jugyō wa ___ hajimarimasu ka?) いつ (itsu) - When does the class start?
  5. ＿＿＿が好きですか。 (___ ga suki desu ka?) 何 (nani) or どれ (dore) - What do you like? or Which one do you like?

Exercise 3: Translate into Japanese

Translate these questions into Japanese:

  1. Where is the station? 駅はどこですか。
    Eki wa doko desu ka?
  2. Do you have a car? 車を持っていますか。
    Kuruma o motte imasu ka?
  3. What time is it now? 今何時ですか。
    Ima nanji desu ka?
  4. Would you like coffee or tea? コーヒーかお茶が欲しいですか。
    Kōhī ka ocha ga hoshii desu ka?
  5. Shall we go together? 一緒に行きましょうか。
    Issho ni ikimashou ka?
