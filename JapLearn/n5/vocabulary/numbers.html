Japanese Numbers

Essential counting system for everyday life

Introduction to Japanese Numbers

The Japanese number system can be a bit complex at first because it uses two different counting systems: the native Japanese numbers (和語 - wago) and Sino-Japanese numbers (漢語 - kango). The native Japanese numbers are typically used for counting up to 10, while the Sino-Japanese system (based on Chinese) is used for most other purposes, especially in formal contexts.

In this lesson, we'll learn both systems and how to count objects using Japanese counters (助数詞 - josūshi), which are special suffixes attached to numbers depending on what is being counted.

Cultural Note: In Japan, certain numbers have cultural significance. The number 4 (四 - shi) is considered unlucky because it sounds like the word for death (死 - shi). Similarly, the number 9 (九 - ku) can be unlucky as it sounds like the word for suffering (苦 - ku). On the other hand, 8 (八 - hachi) is considered lucky because its shape widens from top to bottom, suggesting growth and prosperity.

Basic Numbers (1-10)

Let's start with the numbers 1 through 10 in both the native Japanese and Sino-Japanese systems.

Native Japanese

Important Notes:

  • The native Japanese numbers (hitotsu, futatsu, etc.) are often used when counting objects without using specific counters.
  • The number 4 can be pronounced as either "shi" or "yon". "Yon" is often preferred since "shi" sounds like the word for death.
  • Similarly, 7 can be "shichi" or "nana", and 9 can be "kyū" or "ku". The pronunciation often depends on the counter used.
  • When counting from 1 to 10, Japanese people typically use the Sino-Japanese numbers (ichi, ni, san...).

Pronunciation Changes: Some numbers change pronunciation when combined:

  • 300: 三百 (さんびゃく) sanbyaku (not "san-hyaku")
  • 600: 六百 (ろっぴゃく) roppyaku (not "roku-hyaku")
  • 800: 八百 (はっぴゃく) happyaku (not "hachi-hyaku")
  • 3,000: 三千 (さんぜん) sanzen (not "san-sen")
  • 8,000: 八千 (はっせん) hassen (not "hachi-sen")

Japanese Counters (助数詞)

In Japanese, you need to use specific counters after numbers depending on what you're counting. These are similar to how we say "2 sheets of paper" or "3 pieces of cake" in English, but Japanese has many more of these and they are mandatory.

Counter Japanese Used For Example
〜人 〜にん / 〜り / 〜ひとり
〜nin / 〜ri / 〜hitori		 People 三人 (sannin) - three people
〜つ 〜つ
〜tsu		 General objects 五つ (itsutsu) - five items
〜個 〜こ
〜ko		 Small objects 二個 (niko) - two small items
〜本 〜ほん
〜hon		 Long cylindrical objects 一本 (ippon) - one bottle/pen
〜枚 〜まい
〜mai		 Flat objects 四枚 (yonmai) - four sheets/plates
〜冊 〜さつ
〜satsu		 Books 三冊 (sansatsu) - three books
〜台 〜だい
〜dai		 Machines/vehicles 二台 (nidai) - two cars/computers
〜杯 〜はい
〜hai		 Cups/glasses 一杯 (ippai) - one cup

Examples with Counters:

学生が三人います。

Gakusei ga sannin imasu.

There are three students.

リンゴを二つ食べました。

Ringo o futatsu tabemashita.

I ate two apples.

ビールを一杯ください。

Bīru o ippai kudasai.

One beer, please.

切手を十枚買いました。

Kitte o jūmai kaimashita.

I bought ten stamps.

Pronunciation Changes: Some numbers change pronunciation when combined with counters:

  • 1 + hon → 一本 (いっぽん) ippon
  • 3 + nin → 三人 (さんにん) sannin
  • 6 + satsu → 六冊 (ろくさつ) rokusatsu
  • 8 + hai → 八杯 (はっぱい) happai
  • 10 + ko → 十個 (じゅっこ) jukko

Exercise 2: Using Counters

Choose the correct counter for each item:

  1. 3 books: 本 ___ or 冊 ___ or 個 ___?
  2. 2 dogs: 人 ___ or 匹 ___ or 台 ___?
  3. 5 cars: 台 ___ or 枚 ___ or 杯 ___?
  4. 1 pen: 本 ___ or 個 ___ or 冊 ___?
  5. 4 people: 個 ___ or 人 ___ or 匹 ___?
  1. 三冊 (sansatsu) - 冊 is used for books
  2. 二匹 (nihiki) - 匹 is used for small animals
  3. 五台 (godai) - 台 is used for machines/vehicles
  4. 一本 (ippon) - 本 is used for long cylindrical objects
  5. 四人 (yonin) - 人 is used for people
