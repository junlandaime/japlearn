Understanding Transitive and Intransitive Verbs

N4 Level

One of the most important grammatical distinctions in Japanese is between transitive (他動詞・たどうし) and intransitive (自動詞・じどうし) verbs. This distinction is more systematic and important in Japanese than in English, and understanding it is crucial for forming natural Japanese sentences.

Key Point: In Japanese, many verbs come in transitive-intransitive pairs that have related meanings but different grammatical functions. Learning these pairs together will help you master Japanese sentence structure.

Transitive Verbs (他動詞・たどうし)

Transitive verbs describe an action that is done to something or someone. They take a direct object, which is marked by the particle を (o).

Example:

私はドアを開けます。

Watashi wa doa o akemasu.

I open the door.

In this example, "開ける" (akeru - to open) is a transitive verb. The action of opening is done to the door (the direct object).

Intransitive Verbs (自動詞・じどうし)

Intransitive verbs describe an action or state that does not directly act on an object. They do not take a direct object with を (o).

Example:

ドアが開きます。

Doa ga akimasu.

The door opens.

In this example, "開く" (aku - to open) is an intransitive verb. The door itself performs the action of opening, rather than being acted upon.

Key Differences

Feature Transitive Verbs (他動詞) Intransitive Verbs (自動詞)
Particle Used を (o) for direct object が (ga) for subject
Agency Someone does the action to something Something happens on its own
Focus Focus on the doer of the action Focus on the event or change of state
English Translation Often translated as "do something" Often translated as "something happens"
