Japanese Family Terms

Essential vocabulary for talking about family in Japanese

Introduction to Japanese Family Terms

Family (家族 - kazoku) is a central part of Japanese culture and society. The Japanese language has an extensive vocabulary for referring to family members, with different terms depending on whether you're talking about your own family or someone else's.

Unlike English, Japanese has separate words for "older brother," "younger brother," "older sister," and "younger sister." Additionally, there are different terms when referring to your own family members versus when referring to someone else's family members. This distinction reflects the importance of social hierarchy and relationships in Japanese culture.

Cultural Note: In Japanese society, family structure is traditionally hierarchical, with the eldest male often being the head of the household. However, modern Japanese families have evolved, and many now follow more egalitarian structures. The language still reflects some of these traditional distinctions, especially in the terms used for different family members.

Immediate Family Members

These are the basic terms for immediate family members in Japanese. Note the distinction between terms used for your own family (when speaking to others) and terms used for someone else's family (when speaking to the family member directly).

English Japanese (Your Family) Romaji Japanese (Someone Else's) Romaji Audio
Family 家族 Kazoku ご家族 Go-kazoku
Father 父 / お父さん Chichi / Otōsan お父さん Otōsan
Mother 母 / お母さん Haha / Okāsan お母さん Okāsan
Older Brother 兄 / お兄さん Ani / Onīsan お兄さん Onīsan
Older Sister 姉 / お姉さん Ane / Onēsan お姉さん Onēsan
Younger Brother 弟 Otōto 弟さん Otōto-san
Younger Sister 妹 Imōto 妹さん Imōto-san
Husband 夫 / 主人 Otto / Shujin ご主人 Goshujin
Wife 妻 / 家内 Tsuma / Kanai 奥さん Okusan
Son 息子 Musuko 息子さん Musuko-san
Daughter 娘 Musume 娘さん Musume-san
Child 子供 Kodomo お子さん Oko-san

Example Sentences:

私の父は先生です。

Watashi no chichi wa sensei desu.

My father is a teacher.

あなたのお母さんはどこにいますか。

Anata no okāsan wa doko ni imasu ka.

Where is your mother?

私には兄が二人と妹が一人います。

Watashi ni wa ani ga futari to imōto ga hitori imasu.

I have two older brothers and one younger sister.

Example Sentences:

祖父と祖母は田舎に住んでいます。

Sofu to sobo wa inaka ni sunde imasu.

My grandfather and grandmother live in the countryside.

叔父は医者です。

Oji wa isha desu.

My uncle is a doctor.

In-Laws and Other Family Relations

Japanese has specific terms for in-laws and other family relations. These terms often add prefixes or suffixes to the basic family terms.

English Japanese Romaji Notes Audio
Father-in-law 義父 Gifu 義 (gi) is the prefix for in-laws
Mother-in-law 義母 Gibo Can also be called 姑 (shūtome)
Brother-in-law 義兄 / 義弟 Gikei / Gitei Depends if older or younger than you
Sister-in-law 義姉 / 義妹 Giane / Gimai Depends if older or younger than you
Son-in-law 婿 Muko Also can be 義理の息子 (giri no musuko)
Daughter-in-law 嫁 Yome Also can be 義理の娘 (giri no musume)
Parents-in-law 義両親 Giryōshin Both father-in-law and mother-in-law
Stepfather 継父 Keifu 継 (kei) is the prefix for step-relations
Stepmother 継母 Keibo Also commonly called ままはは (mamahaha)

Example Sentences:

義父と義母は親切です。

Gifu to gibo wa shinsetsu desu.

My father-in-law and mother-in-law are kind.

姉の夫は私の義兄です。

Ane no otto wa watashi no gikei desu.

My sister's husband is my brother-in-law.

Cultural Note: When referring to your own family members in conversation with others, it's customary to use humble language (e.g., 父 chichi for "my father"). When referring to someone else's family members, it's polite to use honorific language (e.g., お父さん otōsan for "your father"). This reflects the Japanese concept of showing respect to others and humility about oneself.
