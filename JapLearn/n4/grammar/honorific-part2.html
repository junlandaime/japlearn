Sonkeigo (尊敬語) - Respectful Language

Sonkeigo is used to show respect to the person you're talking about. It elevates the status of that person by using special honorific forms of verbs and expressions.

How to Form Sonkeigo

There are several ways to form sonkeigo:

  1. Using special honorific verbs
    Plain Form Sonkeigo Form Meaning
    言う (iu) おっしゃる (ossharu) to say
    食べる (taberu) 召し上がる (meshiagaru) to eat
    飲む (nomu) 召し上がる (meshiagaru) to drink
    見る (miru) ご覧になる (goran ni naru) to see/look
    知る (shiru) ご存知だ (gozonji da) to know
    行く/来る (iku/kuru) いらっしゃる (irassharu) to go/come
    いる (iru) いらっしゃる (irassharu) to be (for people)
    する (suru) なさる (nasaru) to do
    もらう (morau) お受け取りになる (o-uketori ni naru) to receive
  2. Using the pattern: お/ご + Verb Stem + になる

    For verbs that don't have special honorific forms, you can use this pattern:

    読む → お読みになる

    yomu → o-yomi ni naru

    to read (respectful)

    書く → お書きになる

    kaku → o-kaki ni naru

    to write (respectful)

    Use お for Japanese-origin verbs (訓読み/kun-yomi) and ご for Chinese-origin verbs (音読み/on-yomi).

Note: Sonkeigo is used when talking about someone of higher status (your boss, teacher, customer, etc.) to a third party or directly to them. It's never used to talk about yourself or your in-group (family, close colleagues).

Examples of Sonkeigo in Context

Plain Form:

先生は今、本を読んでいます。

Sensei wa ima, hon o yonde imasu.

The teacher is reading a book now.

Sonkeigo:

先生は今、本をお読みになっています。

Sensei wa ima, hon o o-yomi ni natte imasu.

The teacher is reading a book now. (respectful)

Plain Form:

社長は明日、東京に行きます。

Shachō wa ashita, Tōkyō ni ikimasu.

The company president will go to Tokyo tomorrow.

Sonkeigo:

社長は明日、東京にいらっしゃいます。

Shachō wa ashita, Tōkyō ni irasshaimasu.

The company president will go to Tokyo tomorrow. (respectful)

Plain Form:

先生は何を食べましたか。

Sensei wa nani o tabemashita ka.

What did the teacher eat?

Sonkeigo:

先生は何を召し上がりましたか。

Sensei wa nani o meshiagarimashita ka.

What did the teacher eat? (respectful)

Kenjougo (謙譲語) - Humble Language

Kenjougo is used to humble yourself or your in-group (family, colleagues, etc.) when talking to or about someone of higher status. It shows respect by lowering your own position rather than elevating the other person's.

How to Form Kenjougo

There are several ways to form kenjougo:

  1. Using special humble verbs
    Plain Form Kenjougo Form Meaning
    言う (iu) 申し上げる (mōshiageru) to say
    食べる/飲む (taberu/nomu) いただく (itadaku) to eat/drink
    見る (miru) 拝見する (haiken suru) to see/look
    知る (shiru) 存じる (zonjiru) to know
    行く (iku) 参る (mairu) to go
    来る (kuru) 参る (mairu) to come
    いる (iru) おる (oru) to be (for people)
    する (suru) いたす (itasu) to do
    あげる (ageru) 差し上げる (sashiageru) to give
    もらう (morau) いただく (itadaku) to receive
    会う (au) お目にかかる (o-me ni kakaru) to meet
  2. Using the pattern: お/ご + Verb Stem + する

    For verbs that don't have special humble forms, you can use this pattern:

    待つ → お待ちする

    matsu → o-machi suru

    to wait (humble)

    手伝う → お手伝いする

    tetsudau → o-tetsudai suru

    to help (humble)

    As with sonkeigo, use お for Japanese-origin verbs and ご for Chinese-origin verbs.

Note: Kenjougo is used when talking about yourself or your in-group to someone of higher status. It's never used to talk about the person you're addressing or someone of higher status.

Examples of Kenjougo in Context

Plain Form:

私は先生に本を見せました。

Watashi wa sensei ni hon o misemashita.

I showed the book to the teacher.

Kenjougo:

私は先生に本をお見せしました。

Watashi wa sensei ni hon o o-mise shimashita.

I showed the book to the teacher. (humble)

Plain Form:

私は明日、会社に行きます。

Watashi wa ashita, kaisha ni ikimasu.

I will go to the company tomorrow.

Kenjougo:

私は明日、会社に参ります。

Watashi wa ashita, kaisha ni mairimasu.

I will go to the company tomorrow. (humble)

Plain Form:

私はこの件について知っています。

Watashi wa kono ken ni tsuite shitte imasu.

I know about this matter.

Kenjougo:

私はこの件について存じております。

Watashi wa kono ken ni tsuite zonjite orimasu.

I know about this matter. (humble)
