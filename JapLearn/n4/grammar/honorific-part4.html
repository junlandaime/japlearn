Common Mistakes

Using Sonkeigo for Yourself

Incorrect:

私は明日、会社にいらっしゃいます。

Watashi wa ashita, kaisha ni irasshaimasu.

(Trying to say "I will come to the company tomorrow.")

Correct:

私は明日、会社に参ります。

Watashi wa ashita, kaisha ni mairimasu.

I will come to the company tomorrow.

Sonkeigo is used to elevate the status of others, not yourself. When talking about your own actions to someone of higher status, use kenjougo (humble language) instead.

Using Kenjougo for Others of Higher Status

Incorrect:

先生は図書館に参ります。

Sensei wa toshokan ni mairimasu.

(Trying to say "The teacher will go to the library.")

Correct:

先生は図書館にいらっしゃいます。

Sensei wa toshokan ni irasshaimasu.

The teacher will go to the library.

Kenjougo is used to humble yourself or your in-group, not to talk about people of higher status. When talking about someone of higher status, use sonkeigo (respectful language) instead.

Mixing Up Special Verbs

Incorrect:

お客様は何を召し上がりますか。

Okyaku-sama wa nani o meshiagarimasu ka.

(Using sonkeigo for "eat")

私は先生にお伝えになります。

Watashi wa sensei ni o-tsutae ni narimasu.

(Using sonkeigo pattern for "tell" about yourself)

Correct:

お客様は何を召し上がりますか。

Okyaku-sama wa nani o meshiagarimasu ka.

(Correct use of sonkeigo for "eat" about a customer)

私は先生にお伝えいたします。

Watashi wa sensei ni o-tsutae itashimasu.

(Using kenjougo pattern for "tell" about yourself)

It's important to remember which special verbs are used for sonkeigo and which are used for kenjougo. Using the wrong form can lead to awkward or inappropriate expressions.

Over-using Keigo

Unnatural:

友達に「明日、お時間がございましたら、ご一緒にお食事をいただければと存じます。」

Tomodachi ni "Ashita, o-jikan ga gozaimashitara, go-issho ni o-shokuji o itadakereba to zonjimasu."

(Using very formal keigo with a friend)

Natural:

友達に「明日、時間があれば、一緒に食事しない？」

Tomodachi ni "Ashita, jikan ga areba, issho ni shokuji shinai?"

(Using casual language with a friend)

Using too much keigo in situations where it's not required can sound unnatural or create distance. With friends and family, casual language is more appropriate. Keigo should be used according to the social context.

Incorrect Prefix Usage (お/ご)

Incorrect:

お質問

o-shitsumon

(Using お with a Chinese-origin word)

ご手紙

go-tegami

(Using ご with a Japanese-origin word)

Correct:

ご質問

go-shitsumon

(Using ご with a Chinese-origin word)

お手紙

o-tegami

(Using お with a Japanese-origin word)

Generally, use お for words of Japanese origin (訓読み/kun-yomi) and ご for words of Chinese origin (音読み/on-yomi). However, there are many exceptions, so it's best to learn common expressions as set phrases.

Summary

In this lesson, we've learned about the three main types of honorific language (keigo) in Japanese:

  • Teineigo (丁寧語) - Polite Language:
    • Uses です/ます forms
    • Used in general formal situations
    • The basic level of politeness
  • Sonkeigo (尊敬語) - Respectful Language:
    • Special verbs like いらっしゃる, 召し上がる, etc.
    • Pattern: お/ご + Verb Stem + になる
    • Used to elevate others of higher status
  • Kenjougo (謙譲語) - Humble Language:
    • Special verbs like 参る, 申し上げる, etc.
    • Pattern: お/ご + Verb Stem + する
    • Used to humble yourself when speaking to those of higher status

The choice of keigo depends on the relationship between the speaker, the listener, and the subject of the conversation. Using appropriate keigo is essential for proper communication in Japanese society and shows your understanding of Japanese social hierarchies and cultural norms.

At the N4 level, you should be able to recognize basic keigo forms and understand their appropriate usage contexts. As you continue to study Japanese, you'll develop a more nuanced understanding of keigo and be able to use it more naturally in various social situations.
