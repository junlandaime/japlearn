Plain Imperative Form

The plain imperative form is the most direct and forceful way to give commands in Japanese. It's considered very strong and can sound rude in many contexts, so it should be used with caution.

How to Form the Plain Imperative

The formation of the plain imperative depends on the type of verb:

Group 1: U-Verbs (五段動詞)

For u-verbs, replace the final ~u with ~e.

Dictionary form: 最後の「う」→「え」

Dictionary Form Imperative Form Example
話す (はなす)
to speak		 話せ (はなせ) 日本語で話せ！
Speak in Japanese!
書く (かく)
to write		 書け (かけ) ここに名前を書け！
Write your name here!
待つ (まつ)
to wait		 待て (まて) そこで待て！
Wait there!
読む (よむ)
to read		 読め (よめ) この本を読め！
Read this book!
飲む (のむ)
to drink		 飲め (のめ) この薬を飲め！
Drink this medicine!

Group 2: RU-Verbs (一段動詞)

For ru-verbs, remove the final ~ru and add ~ろ.

Dictionary form: 「る」を取って、「ろ」を付ける

Dictionary Form Imperative Form Example
食べる (たべる)
to eat		 食べろ (たべろ) 早く食べろ！
Eat quickly!
見る (みる)
to see/look		 見ろ (みろ) あそこを見ろ！
Look over there!
起きる (おきる)
to wake up		 起きろ (おきろ) 早く起きろ！
Wake up early!
出る (でる)
to go out		 出ろ (でろ) ここから出ろ！
Get out of here!

Group 3: Irregular Verbs (不規則動詞)

The two irregular verbs have their own imperative forms:

Dictionary Form Imperative Form Example
する
to do		 しろ 早くしろ！
Hurry up! (lit. Do quickly!)
来る (くる)
to come		 来い (こい) ここに来い！
Come here!

Note: The plain imperative form is rarely used in everyday conversation. It's mainly found in emergency situations, military commands, or in fiction like manga and anime. Using it in normal conversation can sound extremely rude or aggressive.

Appropriate Contexts for Plain Imperative

While the plain imperative form is generally avoided in everyday conversation, there are specific contexts where it is appropriate or commonly used:

1. Emergency Situations

逃げろ！

Nigero!

Run away! (in a dangerous situation)

助けて！

Tasukete!

Help! (This is actually the te-form used as an imperative)

2. Military or Police Commands

止まれ！

Tomare!

Stop! (as might be shouted by police)

気をつけ！

Ki o tsuke!

Attention! (military command)

3. In Fiction (Manga, Anime, etc.)

黙れ！

Damare!

Shut up! (often used by angry characters)

死ね！

Shine!

Die! (extremely harsh, only in fiction)

4. Set Phrases and Expressions

頑張れ！

Ganbare!

Do your best! (cheering at sports events)

気を付けろ！

Ki o tsukero!

Be careful! (warning of danger)

Polite Request Forms

In contrast to the plain imperative, Japanese has several polite forms for making requests or giving instructions. These forms are much more commonly used in everyday conversation.

〜てください Form

The 〜てください form is the standard polite way to make requests in Japanese. It's formed by using the te-form of the verb followed by ください (kudasai).

Verb (て-form) + ください

ドアを開けてください。

Doa o akete kudasai.

Please open the door.

ここに名前を書いてください。

Koko ni namae o kaite kudasai.

Please write your name here.

静かにしてください。

Shizuka ni shite kudasai.

Please be quiet.

明日の朝9時に来てください。

Ashita no asa ku-ji ni kite kudasai.

Please come at 9 AM tomorrow morning.

This form is appropriate in most situations and is the safest choice when you're unsure which level of politeness to use. It's commonly used:

  • When speaking to strangers
  • In customer service situations
  • When making requests to people you don't know well
  • In formal or business settings

〜なさい Form

The 〜なさい form is used by people in positions of authority when giving instructions to those of lower status, particularly by parents to children or teachers to students. It's formed by adding なさい (nasai) to the verb stem (masu-form without ます).

Verb stem (ます-form without ます) + なさい

早く食べなさい。

Hayaku tabenasai.

Eat quickly. (parent to child)

宿題をしなさい。

Shukudai o shinasai.

Do your homework. (teacher to student)

手を洗いなさい。

Te o arainasai.

Wash your hands. (parent to child)

ここに座りなさい。

Koko ni suwarinasai.

Sit here. (teacher to student)

Note: While 〜なさい is more polite than the plain imperative, it still implies authority and should not be used with people of equal or higher status. It's often used with a softer tone of voice than the plain imperative.

〜て Form as a Request

The simple て-form of a verb can be used as a casual request among friends and family. It's less formal than 〜てください but not as harsh as the plain imperative.

Verb (て-form)

ちょっと待って。

Chotto matte.

Wait a moment. (casual)

これ見て。

Kore mite.

Look at this. (casual)

早く来て。

Hayaku kite.

Come quickly. (casual)

手伝って。

Tetsudatte.

Help me. (casual)

This form is commonly used:

  • Among friends
  • Between family members
  • In casual situations
  • When making simple, everyday requests

Tip: Adding ちょっと (chotto, "a little") or ね (ne) at the end can soften the request: 「ちょっと待ってね」 (Chotto matte ne - "Wait a moment, okay?")
