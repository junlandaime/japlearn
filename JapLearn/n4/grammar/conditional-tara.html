Conditional Form (〜たら)

Learn how to express conditions and temporal sequences in Japanese

Introduction to the 〜たら Conditional

The 〜たら conditional is one of the most versatile and commonly used conditional forms in Japanese. It is used to express conditions that must be fulfilled before something else can happen, often translated as "if" or "when" in English.

雨が降ったら、傘を持って行きます。

Ame ga futtara, kasa o motte ikimasu.

If it rains, I will take an umbrella.

Unlike some other conditional forms in Japanese, the 〜たら form can express both hypothetical conditions and temporal sequences. This makes it particularly useful for a wide range of situations.

Key Features

  • • Expresses both hypothetical conditions and temporal sequences
  • • Can be translated as "if" or "when" depending on context
  • • Formed by adding ら to the past tense form
  • • Works with all types of words (verbs, adjectives, nouns)
  • • More versatile than other conditional forms

When to Use 〜たら

  • • For specific, one-time conditions
  • • To express a sequence of events
  • • When describing discoveries or realizations
  • • For hypothetical situations
  • • When giving advice or suggestions

Important Note

Japanese has four main conditional forms: 〜たら, 〜ば, 〜と, and 〜なら. Each has its own nuances and usage patterns. The 〜たら form is often the most versatile and easiest to use, making it a good starting point for learners.

How to Form the 〜たら Conditional

The 〜たら conditional is formed by adding ら to the past (た) form of verbs, adjectives, and nouns with the copula. This makes it relatively simple to form compared to some other conditional forms.

For Verbs

To form the 〜たら conditional with verbs, you need to use the past tense (た form) and add ら:

Verb (past tense) + ら

Group 1: U-Verbs (五段動詞)

Dictionary Form Past Form 〜たら Form
話す (はなす) 話した (はなした) 話したら (はなしたら)
買う (かう) 買った (かった) 買ったら (かったら)
読む (よむ) 読んだ (よんだ) 読んだら (よんだら)
帰る (かえる) 帰った (かえった) 帰ったら (かえったら)

Group 2: RU-Verbs (一段動詞)

Dictionary Form Past Form 〜たら Form
食べる (たべる) 食べた (たべた) 食べたら (たべたら)
見る (みる) 見た (みた) 見たら (みたら)
起きる (おきる) 起きた (おきた) 起きたら (おきたら)
出る (でる) 出た (でた) 出たら (でたら)

Group 3: Irregular Verbs (不規則動詞)

Dictionary Form Past Form 〜たら Form
する (する) した (した) したら (したら)
来る (くる) 来た (きた) 来たら (きたら)

For Adjectives

For adjectives, you also use the past tense form and add ら:

I-Adjectives (い形容詞)

Change the final い to かった + ら

Dictionary Form Past Form 〜たら Form
高い (たかい) 高かった (たかかった) 高かったら (たかかったら)
安い (やすい) 安かった (やすかった) 安かったら (やすかったら)
寒い (さむい) 寒かった (さむかった) 寒かったら (さむかったら)

NA-Adjectives (な形容詞)

Add だった + ら

Dictionary Form Past Form 〜たら Form
静か (しずか) 静かだった (しずかだった) 静かだったら (しずかだったら)
便利 (べんり) 便利だった (べんりだった) 便利だったら (べんりだったら)
元気 (げんき) 元気だった (げんきだった) 元気だったら (げんきだったら)

For Nouns

For nouns, you add だったら (the past tense of the copula だ plus ら):

Noun + だったら

Noun 〜たら Form
学生 (がくせい) 学生だったら (がくせいだったら)
日曜日 (にちようび) 日曜日だったら (にちようびだったら)
先生 (せんせい) 先生だったら (せんせいだったら)

Negative 〜たら Form

To create the negative 〜たら form, you use the negative past tense form:

For Verbs

Use the negative past form + ら

行かなかった → 行かなかったら

Ikanakatta → Ikanakattara

If (I/you/etc.) don't go

For I-Adjectives

Use the negative past form + ら

高くなかった → 高くなかったら

Takakunakatta → Takakunakattara

If (it) is not expensive

For NA-Adjectives

Use the negative past form + ら

静かじゃなかった → 静かじゃなかったら

Shizuka janakatta → Shizuka janakattara

If (it) is not quiet

For Nouns

Use the negative past form + ら

学生じゃなかった → 学生じゃなかったら

Gakusei janakatta → Gakusei janakattara

If (he/she) is not a student

Note: The 〜たら form is easier to form than the 〜ば form because it consistently follows the pattern of past tense + ら for all word types. This makes it a very practical conditional form to learn and use.

Usage Patterns

The 〜たら conditional in Japanese has several important usage patterns. Let's explore the most common ways to use this grammar:

1. Temporal Sequence

The 〜たら form is commonly used to express a sequence of events, where one action happens after another. In this usage, it's often translated as "when" or "after" rather than "if."

Example:

家に帰ったら、宿題をします。

Ie ni kaettara, shukudai o shimasu.

When I get home, I'll do my homework.

Example:

食事が終わったら、映画を見ましょう。

Shokuji ga owattara, eiga o mimashou.

After the meal is finished, let's watch a movie.

In these examples, the second action will occur after the first action is completed. This temporal relationship is a key feature of the 〜たら form.

2. Specific Conditions

The 〜たら form is used to express specific, one-time conditions, rather than general rules or principles.

Example:

明日雨が降ったら、傘を持って行きます。

Ashita ame ga futtara, kasa o motte ikimasu.

If it rains tomorrow, I'll take an umbrella.

Example:

この薬を飲んだら、すぐに良くなるでしょう。

Kono kusuri o nondara, sugu ni yoku naru deshou.

If you take this medicine, you'll probably feel better soon.

Note: While the 〜ば form is often used for general rules or natural consequences, the 〜たら form is better for specific situations or one-time events.

3. Discoveries and Realizations

The 〜たら form is often used to express unexpected discoveries or realizations. In this usage, the past tense is commonly used in the main clause.

Example:

家に帰ったら、誰もいませんでした。

Ie ni kaettara, dare mo imasen deshita.

When I got home, there was nobody there.

Example:

財布を見たら、お金がなくなっていました。

Saifu o mitara, okane ga nakunatte imashita.

When I looked at my wallet, my money was gone.

This usage often conveys a sense of surprise or an unexpected situation that was discovered.

4. Hypothetical Situations

The 〜たら form can be used to express hypothetical situations, including counterfactual conditions (things that didn't or won't happen).

Example:

お金があったら、新しい車を買うのに。

Okane ga attara, atarashii kuruma o kau noni.

If I had money, I would buy a new car.

Example:

日本に行ったら、何をしたいですか。

Nihon ni ittara, nani o shitai desu ka.

If you went to Japan, what would you want to do?

5. Giving Advice

The 〜たら form can be used when giving advice or making suggestions.

Example:

頭が痛かったら、この薬を飲んでください。

Atama ga itakattara, kono kusuri o nonde kudasai.

If you have a headache, please take this medicine.

Example:

分からなかったら、先生に聞いてみたらどうですか。

Wakaranakattara, sensei ni kiite mitara dou desu ka.

If you don't understand, how about asking the teacher?

Common Expressions with 〜たら

There are several fixed expressions and patterns that use the 〜たら form:

〜たらどうですか

Used for making suggestions

もっと早く起きたらどうですか。

Motto hayaku okitara dou desu ka.

How about waking up earlier?

〜たらいいですか

Used for asking for advice or permission

何を買ったらいいですか。

Nani o kattara ii desu ka.

What should I buy?

〜たらいいのに

Used for expressing regret or wishful thinking

もっと勉強したらいいのに。

Motto benkyou shitara ii noni.

You should study more. (It would be good if you studied more.)

〜たら〜たで

Used for expressing that either way, there's a problem

行ったら行ったで、問題があります。

Ittara itta de, mondai ga arimasu.

Even if I go, there will still be problems.

Comparing 〜たら with Other Conditional Forms

Japanese has four main conditional forms, each with its own nuances. Here's how 〜たら compares to the others:

Form Main Usage Example
〜たら Temporal sequence, specific one-time conditions, discoveries 家に帰ったら、電話します。
When I get home, I'll call you.
〜ば Natural consequences, general truths, hypothetical situations 毎日勉強すれば、上手になります。
If you study every day, you'll improve.
〜と Automatic or natural results, definite outcomes このボタンを押すと、機械が動きます。
If you press this button, the machine will move.
〜なら Contextual conditions, introducing new information 日本語を勉強するなら、この本がいいです。
If you're studying Japanese, this book is good.

The 〜たら form is perhaps the most versatile of all the conditional forms, as it can be used in a wide range of situations. It's especially useful for beginners because it follows a consistent formation pattern and can express many different types of conditions.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Confusing 〜たら with 〜ば for general rules

❌ 毎日運動したら、健康になります。

Mainichi undou shitara, kenkou ni narimasu.

✓ 毎日運動すれば、健康になります。

Mainichi undou sureba, kenkou ni narimasu.

For general rules or principles that always apply, the 〜ば form is more appropriate than 〜たら.

Mistake 2: Using 〜たら for commands

❌ ここに来たら。

Koko ni kitara.

✓ ここに来てください。

Koko ni kite kudasai.

The 〜たら form cannot be used by itself as a command. Use the appropriate command forms instead.

Mistake 3: Using 〜たら with the wrong tense in the main clause

❌ 明日、雨が降ったら、試合は中止でした。

Ashita, ame ga futtara, shiai wa chuushi deshita.

✓ 明日、雨が降ったら、試合は中止です。

Ashita, ame ga futtara, shiai wa chuushi desu.

When talking about future conditions, use the present tense in the main clause, not the past tense.

Summary

Key Takeaways

  • • The 〜たら conditional form is used to express temporal sequences, specific conditions, discoveries, and hypothetical situations.
  • • It is formed by adding ら to the past tense form of verbs, adjectives, and nouns with the copula.
  • • The 〜たら form is more versatile than the 〜ば form and can be used in a wider range of situations.
  • • It is particularly useful for expressing a sequence of events ("when" or "after") and specific, one-time conditions.
  • • The 〜たら form is one of four conditional forms in Japanese, each with its own nuances and usage patterns.
