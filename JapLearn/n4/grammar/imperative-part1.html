Introduction to the Imperative Form

The imperative form in Japanese is used to give commands, make requests, or issue instructions. Unlike English, which primarily uses one form for commands ("Go!", "Stop!", etc.), Japanese has several different ways to express commands, each with varying levels of politeness and formality.

窓を開けろ！

Mado o akero!

Open the window! (Strong command)

窓を開けてください。

Mado o akete kudasai.

Please open the window. (Polite request)

Understanding the different imperative forms and when to use them is crucial for effective communication in Japanese. Using the wrong form can make you sound rude or overly formal, depending on the situation.

Types of Imperative Forms

  • • Plain Imperative: Direct commands (casual or harsh)
  • • 〜てください Form: Polite requests
  • • 〜なさい Form: Used by superiors to subordinates
  • • 〜て Form: Casual requests among friends
  • • 〜てくれる？ Form: Asking for favors
  • • 〜ていただけませんか Form: Very polite requests

When to Use Each Form

  • • Plain Imperative: Emergency situations, military commands, manga/anime
  • • 〜てください: Standard polite requests in most situations
  • • 〜なさい: Parents to children, teachers to students
  • • 〜て Form: Among close friends and family
  • • 〜てくれる？: Casual requests with friends
  • • 〜ていただけませんか: Formal situations, with superiors

Important Warning

The plain imperative form (〜ろ/〜え) is considered very harsh and can be offensive if used incorrectly. It's generally only appropriate in specific contexts like emergencies, military settings, or when depicted in manga/anime. In everyday conversation, it's better to use more polite forms unless you're very familiar with Japanese culture and language nuances.
