Passive Sentence Structure

The structure of passive sentences in Japanese differs from active sentences. Let's examine how to construct passive sentences:

Basic Passive Structure

Active Sentence

先生が生徒を褒めました。

Sensei ga seito o homemashita.

The teacher praised the student.

Passive Sentence

生徒が先生に褒められました。

Seito ga sensei ni homeraremashita.

The student was praised by the teacher.

Passive Sentence Structure:

Subject (receiver) + が + Agent (doer) + に + Verb (passive form)

In passive sentences:

  • The object of the active sentence becomes the subject of the passive sentence.
  • The subject of the active sentence becomes the agent marked by the particle に.
  • The verb changes to its passive form.

Note: Sometimes the agent (doer) can be omitted if it's clear from context or unimportant.

Passive with Different Particles

In some cases, particles other than に may be used to mark the agent in passive sentences:

Particle Usage Example
に Most common, used with animate agents (people, animals) 私は友達に招待されました。
Watashi wa tomodachi ni shōtai saremashita.
I was invited by my friend.
によって Used with inanimate agents or for emphasis この橋は台風によって破壊されました。
Kono hashi wa taifū ni yotte hakai saremashita.
This bridge was destroyed by the typhoon.
から Used to indicate the source of an action 私は先生から褒められました。
Watashi wa sensei kara homeraremashita.
I received praise from the teacher.

The choice of particle can slightly change the nuance of the sentence, but に is the most common and versatile option.

Types of Passive in Japanese

As mentioned in the introduction, there are three main types of passive in Japanese. Let's explore each type in more detail:

1. Direct Passive (直接受身 - Chokusetsu Ukemi)

The direct passive is similar to the English passive voice. It's used when the subject of the passive sentence was the direct object of the active sentence.

Active:

先生が私を叱りました。

Sensei ga watashi o shikarimashita.

The teacher scolded me.

Passive:

私は先生に叱られました。

Watashi wa sensei ni shikararemashita.

I was scolded by the teacher.

In direct passive sentences:

  • The object of the active sentence (marked with を) becomes the subject of the passive sentence (marked with は/が).
  • The subject of the active sentence becomes the agent (marked with に).
  • The verb changes to its passive form.

2. Indirect Passive (間接受身 - Kansetsu Ukemi)

The indirect passive is unique to Japanese. It's used when the subject is affected by an action, even though they weren't directly involved in the original action. This type of passive often (but not always) implies that the subject was negatively affected.

私は雨に降られました。

Watashi wa ame ni furaremashita.

I was rained on. (Literally: I was affected by the rain falling.)

In this example, "I" wasn't the direct object of the rain's action (rain doesn't fall "me"), but "I" was affected by the rain falling.

私は子供に泣かれました。

Watashi wa kodomo ni nakaremashita.

I was affected by the child crying. / The child cried on me (troubling me).

私は友達に家に来られました。

Watashi wa tomodachi ni ie ni koraremashita.

I was affected by my friend coming to my house (unexpectedly).

The indirect passive is often used to express inconvenience or annoyance, but it can also be neutral or even positive depending on the context.

3. Suffering Passive (迷惑受身 - Meiwaku Ukemi)

The suffering passive is a special type of indirect passive that specifically expresses that the subject was negatively affected by someone's action. It always carries a negative connotation.

私は財布を盗まれました。

Watashi wa saifu o nusumaremashita.

I had my wallet stolen. / My wallet was stolen (from me).

私は彼に嘘をつかれました。

Watashi wa kare ni uso o tsukaremashita.

I was lied to by him. / He lied to me (and I suffered because of it).

Note: In suffering passive sentences, the thing that was affected (like "wallet" in the first example) is marked with を, not が or は. This is a key characteristic of the suffering passive.
