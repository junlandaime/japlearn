Practice Exercises

Test your understanding of the imperative forms with these practice exercises.

Exercise 1: Plain Imperative Form

Convert the following verbs to their plain imperative form.

1. 行く (いく) - to go

行け (いけ)

Ike - Go! (U-verb: change final う to え)

2. 食べる (たべる) - to eat

食べろ (たべろ)

Tabero - Eat! (RU-verb: remove る and add ろ)

3. 聞く (きく) - to listen/ask

聞け (きけ)

Kike - Listen! (U-verb: change final う to え)

4. 見る (みる) - to see/look

見ろ (みろ)

Miro - Look! (RU-verb: remove る and add ろ)

5. する - to do

しろ

Shiro - Do! (Irregular verb)

6. 来る (くる) - to come

来い (こい)

Koi - Come! (Irregular verb)

Exercise 2: Choosing the Right Form

Choose the most appropriate imperative form for each situation.

1. You want to ask a stranger to take your photo. Which form would you use?

写真を撮ってもらえませんか。 / 写真を撮ってくださいませんか。

Shashin o totte moraemasen ka. / Shashin o totte kudasaimasen ka.

When speaking to a stranger, a polite form like 〜てもらえませんか or 〜てくださいませんか is appropriate.

2. A mother telling her child to clean their room. Which form would be appropriate?

部屋を掃除しなさい。

Heya o souji shinasai.

The 〜なさい form is commonly used by parents when giving instructions to their children.

3. You're asking a close friend to wait for you. Which form would you use?

ちょっと待って。 / 待ってくれる？

Chotto matte. / Matte kureru?

With close friends, the casual て-form or 〜てくれる？ form is appropriate.

4. A police officer ordering someone to stop during an emergency. Which form would be used?

止まれ！

Tomare!

In emergency situations or by authorities giving commands, the plain imperative form is used.

5. You're asking your boss for time off. Which form would be most appropriate?

休みを取らせていただけませんか。

Yasumi o torasete itadakemasen ka.

When speaking to superiors, the very polite 〜ていただけませんか form is appropriate.

Exercise 3: Converting to Negative Commands

Convert the following positive commands to negative commands (prohibitions).

1. ここで食べてください。 → (Please don't eat here.)

ここで食べないでください。

Koko de tabenaide kudasai.

2. 入って。 → (Don't come in.)

入らないで。

Hairanaide.

3. 行け！ → (Don't go!)

行くな！

Iku na!

4. 部屋を掃除しなさい。 → (Don't clean the room.)

部屋を掃除しないでください。 / 部屋を掃除しなくていいです。

Heya o souji shinaide kudasai. / Heya o souji shinakute ii desu.

The second option means "You don't have to clean the room" which is often more natural than a direct prohibition in this context.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Using the Plain Imperative in Everyday Conversation

❌ 窓を開けろ！ (to a colleague or stranger)

Mado o akero!

✓ 窓を開けてもらえますか？

Mado o akete moraemasu ka?

The plain imperative form is too harsh for everyday conversation and can sound rude. Use polite request forms instead.

Mistake 2: Using 〜なさい with People of Equal or Higher Status

❌ 先生、ここに座りなさい。

Sensei, koko ni suwarinasai.

✓ 先生、こちらにお座りください。

Sensei, kochira ni osuwari kudasai.

The 〜なさい form is used by people in positions of authority. Using it with equals or superiors is inappropriate.

Mistake 3: Not Softening Requests Appropriately

❌ 手伝ってください。 (abrupt request to a superior)

Tetsudatte kudasai.

✓ 手伝っていただけませんか。

Tetsudatte itadakemasen ka.

When making requests to superiors or in formal situations, it's important to use appropriately polite forms.

Mistake 4: Using the Wrong Prohibition Form

❌ 行くな！ (to a customer or colleague)

Iku na!

✓ 行かないでください。

Ikanaide kudasai.

The 〜な prohibition form is very harsh. In most situations, 〜ないでください is more appropriate.

Summary

Key Takeaways

  • • Japanese has multiple imperative forms, each with different levels of politeness and appropriate contexts for use.
  • • The plain imperative (〜ろ/〜え) is very direct and should be used with caution, mainly in emergencies or fiction.
  • • The 〜てください form is the standard polite way to make requests in most situations.
  • • The 〜なさい form is used by people in authority when giving instructions to subordinates.
  • • The simple て-form can be used for casual requests among friends and family.
  • • More polite forms like 〜ていただけませんか are appropriate when speaking to superiors or in formal situations.
  • • Negative commands (prohibitions) have their own forms, with 〜ないでください being the standard polite form.
