Japanese Greetings

Essential expressions for everyday conversations

Introduction to Japanese Greetings

Greetings (あいさつ - aisatsu) are an essential part of Japanese culture and daily communication. In Japan, proper greetings show respect and establish good relationships. The Japanese language has specific greetings for different times of day, situations, and levels of formality.

Learning these basic expressions will help you navigate social interactions in Japanese and make a positive impression. Many Japanese greetings come with appropriate bowing gestures, which vary depending on the level of formality.

Cultural Note: In Japanese culture, bowing (お辞儀 - ojigi) accompanies many greetings. The depth and duration of the bow indicate the level of respect. A slight nod is casual, while a deep bow shows profound respect.

Basic Daily Greetings

These are the most common greetings you'll use throughout the day in Japanese.

Japanese Romaji English Usage Audio
おはようございます Ohayō gozaimasu Good morning Formal greeting used in the morning until around 10-11 AM
おはよう Ohayō Morning / Hi Casual morning greeting for friends and family
こんにちは Konnichiwa Good afternoon / Hello All-purpose greeting used from late morning to early evening
こんばんは Konbanwa Good evening Evening greeting used after sunset
さようなら Sayōnara Goodbye Used when parting for a longer period
じゃあね Jā ne See you / Later Casual goodbye when you'll see someone again soon
Formal
Casual
All-purpose

Example Dialogues:

Morning greeting at work:

A: おはようございます。

A: Ohayō gozaimasu.

B: おはようございます。お元気ですか。

B: Ohayō gozaimasu. O-genki desu ka?

Translation: A: Good morning. B: Good morning. How are you?

Evening casual greeting:

A: こんばんは、田中さん。

A: Konbanwa, Tanaka-san.

B: あ、こんばんは、鈴木さん。

B: A, konbanwa, Suzuki-san.

Translation: A: Good evening, Mr. Tanaka. B: Oh, good evening, Mr. Suzuki.

Polite Expressions and Thank You

These expressions are essential for showing politeness and gratitude in Japanese culture.

Japanese Romaji English Usage Audio
ありがとうございます Arigatō gozaimasu Thank you very much Formal expression of gratitude
ありがとう Arigatō Thank you Casual expression of gratitude
どういたしまして Dō itashimashite You're welcome Formal response to thanks
いいえ Iie No / Not at all Casual response to thanks or apologies
すみません Sumimasen Excuse me / I'm sorry / Thank you Multi-purpose polite expression for attention, apologies, or gratitude
失礼します Shitsurei shimasu Excuse me / Pardon me Polite expression when entering/leaving a room or situation

Example Dialogues:

Expressing gratitude:

A: これをどうぞ。

A: Kore o dōzo.

B: ありがとうございます。

B: Arigatō gozaimasu.

A: どういたしまして。

A: Dō itashimashite.

Translation: A: Here you are. B: Thank you very much. A: You're welcome.

Getting someone's attention:

A: すみません、トイレはどこですか。

A: Sumimasen, toire wa doko desu ka?

B: あちらです。

B: Achira desu.

Translation: A: Excuse me, where is the restroom? B: It's over there.

Asking About Well-Being

In Japanese, there are various ways to ask how someone is doing, depending on the level of formality and the relationship between speakers.

Japanese Romaji English Usage Audio
お元気ですか O-genki desu ka How are you? Standard polite way to ask about someone's well-being
お元気でしたか O-genki deshita ka Have you been well? Used when meeting someone after a long time
元気？ Genki? How are you? / Are you good? Casual way to ask friends how they are
調子はどうですか Chōshi wa dō desu ka How are you doing? / How's it going? More casual-polite way to ask about condition
元気です Genki desu I'm fine / I'm well Standard response to "O-genki desu ka"
まあまあです Maa maa desu I'm so-so / I'm okay Honest response when you're not feeling great

Example Dialogue:

A: 山田さん、お久しぶりです。お元気でしたか。

A: Yamada-san, o-hisashiburi desu. O-genki deshita ka?

B: はい、元気です。鈴木さんはどうですか。

B: Hai, genki desu. Suzuki-san wa dō desu ka?

A: おかげさまで元気です。

A: Okagesama de genki desu.

Translation: A: Mr. Yamada, it's been a long time. Have you been well? B: Yes, I'm well. How about you, Mr. Suzuki? A: Thanks to you, I'm doing well.

Cultural Note: The phrase おかげさまで (okagesama de) literally means "thanks to you" and is commonly used when responding that you're doing well. It acknowledges that your well-being is partly due to the support or good wishes of others.
