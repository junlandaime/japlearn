Common Mistakes with Keigo

Even native Japanese speakers sometimes struggle with using keigo correctly. Here are some common mistakes to avoid:

Mixing Sonkeigo and Kenjougo

One of the most common mistakes is mixing sonkeigo and kenjougo in the same sentence when referring to the same person.

Incorrect:

先生はお書きになって、私にお見せになりました。

Sensei wa o-kaki ni natte, watashi ni o-mise ni narimashita.

(Using sonkeigo for both the teacher's actions)

Correct:

先生はお書きになって、私に見せてくださいました。

Sensei wa o-kaki ni natte, watashi ni misete kudasaimashita.

(Using sonkeigo for the teacher's writing action and teineigo with くださる for the action directed toward you)

Using Sonkeigo for Yourself

Never use sonkeigo when talking about yourself or your in-group. This is considered extremely inappropriate.

Incorrect:

私は明日、ご覧になります。

Watashi wa ashita, goran ni narimasu.

(Using sonkeigo for your own action)

Correct:

私は明日、拝見します。

Watashi wa ashita, haiken shimasu.

(Using kenjougo for your own action)

Overusing Keigo

Using too much keigo or using it in inappropriate situations can make you sound unnatural or even sarcastic.

Inappropriate:

友達に「どちらにいらっしゃいますか？」

Tomodachi ni "Dochira ni irasshaimasu ka?"

(Using sonkeigo with a close friend)

Appropriate:

友達に「どこに行く？」

Tomodachi ni "Doko ni iku?"

(Using casual form with a close friend)

Incorrect Verb Forms

Using the wrong verb form or pattern when creating sonkeigo or kenjougo.

Incorrect:

先生は教室にいらっしゃります。

Sensei wa kyōshitsu ni irasshaimasu.

(Incorrect conjugation of いらっしゃる)

Correct:

先生は教室にいらっしゃいます。

Sensei wa kyōshitsu ni irasshaimasu.

(Correct conjugation of いらっしゃる)

Summary

In this lesson, we've learned about the three main types of keigo in Japanese:

  • Teineigo (丁寧語): Basic polite language using です/ます forms, used in general polite conversation.
  • Sonkeigo (尊敬語): Respectful language that elevates the status of the person you're talking about, using special honorific verbs or the お/ご + verb stem + になる pattern.
  • Kenjougo (謙譲語): Humble language that lowers your own status when talking to someone of higher status, using special humble verbs or the お/ご + verb stem + する pattern.

Key points to remember:

  • Use sonkeigo when talking about someone of higher status.
  • Use kenjougo when talking about yourself or your in-group to someone of higher status.
  • Never use sonkeigo for yourself or kenjougo for your superior.
  • The level of keigo you use depends on the social context, your relationship with the person, and the formality of the situation.
  • Keigo is an essential part of Japanese communication and shows your understanding of Japanese social norms and respect.

Mastering keigo takes time and practice. Don't be discouraged if you make mistakes – even native Japanese speakers sometimes struggle with the correct usage. The most important thing is to be aware of the different forms and to make an effort to use them appropriately.

Next Steps

Now that you've learned about honorific language in Japanese, here are some suggestions for further study:

Practice with Real-Life Situations

Try role-playing different scenarios where you need to use keigo, such as a job interview, talking to a teacher, or working in customer service. This will help you get comfortable with using the appropriate forms in context.

Study Business Japanese

Business Japanese (ビジネス日本語) involves a lot of keigo. Look for resources specifically focused on business communication to deepen your understanding of formal Japanese.

Watch Japanese Dramas and Movies

Pay attention to how characters use different levels of politeness depending on their relationships. Workplace dramas are especially good for observing keigo in action.

Explore Related Grammar Points

Continue your study with related grammar points such as giving and receiving (あげる/もらう/くれる), which also reflect the social relationships between people in Japanese culture.
