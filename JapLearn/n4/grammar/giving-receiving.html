Introduction to Giving and Receiving Verbs

In Japanese, there are specific verbs used to express the actions of giving and receiving. These verbs are particularly important because they not only convey the action itself but also indicate the direction of the action and the social relationship between the people involved.

私は友達にプレゼントをあげました。

Watashi wa tomodachi ni purezento o agemashita.

I gave a present to my friend.

Unlike English, which primarily uses "give" and "receive," Japanese has three main verbs for these actions: あげる (ageru), くれる (kureru), and もらう (morau). The choice of which verb to use depends on the direction of the action in relation to the speaker.

Key Verbs

  • • あげる (ageru): To give (from the speaker or speaker's group to others)
  • • くれる (kureru): To give (from others to the speaker or speaker's group)
  • • もらう (morau): To receive (by the speaker or speaker's group from others)

Honorific Forms

  • • さしあげる (sashiageru): Polite form of あげる
  • • くださる (kudasaru): Polite form of くれる
  • • いただく (itadaku): Polite form of もらう

Cultural Note

The giving and receiving verbs in Japanese reflect the importance of social relationships and hierarchy in Japanese culture. Using the correct verb is essential for proper communication and showing respect. These verbs are deeply connected to the concept of "uchi" (in-group) and "soto" (out-group) in Japanese society.

Why Are These Verbs Important?

Direction Matters

Unlike English, Japanese explicitly indicates the direction of giving/receiving through verb choice, making the relationship between giver and receiver clear.

Social Relationships

The choice of verb reflects and reinforces social relationships, showing respect, familiarity, or hierarchy between people.

Beyond Physical Objects

These verbs are used not just for physical items but also for actions done for someone's benefit, making them essential for expressing favors and assistance.

Basic Giving and Receiving Verbs

Let's examine each of the three basic giving and receiving verbs in detail, understanding their usage and the direction of action they represent.

あげる (Ageru) - To Give

あげる is used when the speaker (or someone in the speaker's group) gives something to someone else. The direction of giving is from the speaker to others.

Subject + は + Recipient + に + Object + を + あげる

私は妹にチョコレートをあげました。

Watashi wa imouto ni chokoreeto o agemashita.

I gave chocolate to my younger sister.

田中さんは山田さんに本をあげるつもりです。

Tanaka-san wa Yamada-san ni hon o ageru tsumori desu.

Mr. Tanaka intends to give a book to Mr. Yamada.

Important Points About あげる

  • • Cannot be used when giving to yourself
  • • Used when giving to people outside your "in-group" (family, close friends, colleagues)
  • • Can be used for both physical objects and actions/favors
  • • Conjugates as a regular る-verb

Note: Be careful not to use あげる when talking about giving something to someone of higher status, as it can sound presumptuous. In such cases, use the honorific form さしあげる instead.

Using あげる for Actions (〜てあげる)

Beyond giving physical objects, あげる can be combined with the て-form of verbs to express doing something for someone else's benefit.

Verb (て-form) + あげる

弟の宿題を手伝ってあげました。

Otouto no shukudai o tetsudatte agemashita.

I helped my younger brother with his homework (for his benefit).

道に迷っている人に道を教えてあげました。

Michi ni mayotte iru hito ni michi o oshiete agemashita.

I gave directions to a person who was lost (for their benefit).

Cultural Note: While 〜てあげる is grammatically correct, be careful not to overuse it, especially when talking about yourself. It can sometimes sound like you're boasting about your kindness or generosity, which is considered impolite in Japanese culture.

くれる (Kureru) - To Give

くれる is used when someone else gives something to the speaker (or someone in the speaker's group). The direction of giving is from others to the speaker.

Giver + は + (Recipient + に) + Object + を + くれる

友達は私に誕生日プレゼントをくれました。

Tomodachi wa watashi ni tanjoubi purezento o kuremashita.

My friend gave me a birthday present.

先生は新しい本をくれました。

Sensei wa atarashii hon o kuremashita.

The teacher gave me a new book.

(Note: When the recipient is clearly the speaker, the に phrase can be omitted)

Important Points About くれる

  • • The recipient must be the speaker or someone in the speaker's "in-group"
  • • Expresses a sense of gratitude or appreciation for the giver's action
  • • Can be used for both physical objects and actions/favors
  • • Conjugates as a regular る-verb

Note: くれる is often used to express appreciation for someone else's actions toward you or your group. It emphasizes the kindness of the giver.

Using くれる for Actions (〜てくれる)

Similar to あげる, くれる can be combined with the て-form of verbs to express someone doing something for the benefit of the speaker or the speaker's group.

Verb (て-form) + くれる

母は美味しい料理を作ってくれました。

Haha wa oishii ryouri o tsukutte kuremashita.

My mother made delicious food for me.

友達は空港まで送ってくれました。

Tomodachi wa kuukou made okutte kuremashita.

My friend took me to the airport (for my benefit).

Usage Tip: 〜てくれる is frequently used in Japanese to express gratitude for actions done for you. It's much more common than 〜てあげる in everyday conversation.

もらう (Morau) - To Receive

もらう is used when the speaker (or someone in the speaker's group) receives something from someone else. It describes the same situation as くれる but from the receiver's perspective.

Recipient + は + Giver + に + Object + を + もらう

私は友達に誕生日プレゼントをもらいました。

Watashi wa tomodachi ni tanjoubi purezento o moraimashita.

I received a birthday present from my friend.

弟は祖父にお年玉をもらいました。

Otouto wa sofu ni otoshidama o moraimashita.

My younger brother received New Year's money from our grandfather.

Important Points About もらう

  • • The subject (recipient) must be the speaker or someone in the speaker's "in-group"
  • • The particle に is used to mark the giver (unlike English "from")
  • • Can be used for both physical objects and actions/favors
  • • Conjugates as a regular う-verb

Comparison: While くれる focuses on the giver's kindness, もらう focuses on the receiver's perspective and can express the receiver's initiative in the exchange.

Using もらう for Actions (〜てもらう)

もらう can be combined with the て-form of verbs to express having someone do something for you or receiving the benefit of someone's action.

Recipient + は + Giver + に + Verb (て-form) + もらう

先生に日本語を教えてもらいました。

Sensei ni nihongo o oshiete moraimashita.

I had the teacher teach me Japanese. / I received Japanese lessons from the teacher.

友達に宿題を手伝ってもらいました。

Tomodachi ni shukudai o tetsudatte moraimashita.

I had my friend help me with my homework.

Usage Tip: 〜てもらう often implies that the recipient requested or arranged for the action to be done, unlike 〜てくれる which emphasizes the giver's voluntary action.

Comparison of Basic Giving and Receiving Verbs

Verb Direction Subject Object Marker Recipient/Giver Marker Example
あげる
(ageru)		 Speaker → Others Giver を (o) に (ni) for recipient 私は友達に本をあげました。
I gave a book to my friend.

Honorific Forms of Giving and Receiving Verbs

In Japanese, social hierarchy and politeness are important aspects of communication. The giving and receiving verbs have special honorific forms that are used to show respect or humility depending on the social relationship between the giver and receiver.

Honorific Forms Overview

Basic Form Honorific Form Usage
あげる
(ageru)		 さしあげる
(sashiageru)		 When you give something to someone of higher status
くれる
(kureru)		 くださる
(kudasaru)		 When someone of higher status gives something to you
もらう
(morau)		 いただく
(itadaku)		 When you receive something from someone of higher status

さしあげる (Sashiageru)

さしあげる is the humble form of あげる. It is used when you (or someone in your group) give something to a person of higher status, such as your teacher, boss, or customer.

Subject + は + Recipient(higher status) + に + Object + を + さしあげる

私は先生にお土産をさしあげました。

Watashi wa sensei ni omiyage o sashiagemashita.

I gave a souvenir to my teacher.

秘書は社長にレポートをさしあげました。

Hisho wa shachou ni repooto o sashiagemashita.

The secretary gave the report to the company president.

Using さしあげる for Actions (〜てさしあげる)

Similar to あげる, さしあげる can be combined with the て-form of verbs to express doing something for the benefit of someone of higher status.

お客様に荷物を運んでさしあげました。

Okyaku-sama ni nimotsu o hakonde sashiagemashita.

I carried the luggage for the customer.

くださる (Kudasaru)

くださる is the honorific form of くれる. It is used when someone of higher status gives something to you or someone in your group.

Giver(higher status) + は + (Recipient + に) + Object + を + くださる

先生は私に推薦状を書いてくださいました。

Sensei wa watashi ni suisenjou o kaite kudasaimashita.

The teacher wrote a recommendation letter for me.

社長は社員全員にボーナスをくださいました。

Shachou wa shain zenin ni boonasu o kudasaimashita.

The company president gave a bonus to all employees.

Using くださる for Actions (〜てくださる)

くださる can be combined with the て-form of verbs to express someone of higher status doing something for your benefit.

先生は難しい問題を説明してくださいました。

Sensei wa muzukashii mondai o setsumei shite kudasaimashita.

The teacher explained the difficult problem for me.

Important Note: The form 〜てください is derived from くださる and is used for polite requests, meaning "please do something."

いただく (Itadaku)

いただく is the humble form of もらう. It is used when you (or someone in your group) receive something from a person of higher status.

Recipient + は + Giver(higher status) + に + Object + を + いただく

私は先生にアドバイスをいただきました。

Watashi wa sensei ni adobaisu o itadakimashita.

I received advice from my teacher.

私たちは社長からお祝いのメッセージをいただきました。

Watashitachi wa shachou kara oiwai no messeeji o itadakimashita.

We received a congratulatory message from the company president.

(Note: から can sometimes be used instead of に with いただく)

Using いただく for Actions (〜ていただく)

いただく can be combined with the て-form of verbs to express having someone of higher status do something for your benefit.

先生に日本語を教えていただきました。

Sensei ni nihongo o oshiete itadakimashita.

I had the teacher teach me Japanese. (humble expression)

Cultural Note: The expression 〜ていただけませんか is a very polite way to make requests, meaning "Would you please do something for me?"

Common Expressions with Honorific Forms

Requests with 〜ていただけませんか

Very polite way to ask someone to do something for you

この書類に署名していただけませんか。

Kono shorui ni shomei shite itadakemasen ka.

Would you please sign this document?

Gratitude with 〜てくださってありがとう

Expressing gratitude for someone's action

手伝ってくださってありがとうございます。

Tetsudatte kudasatte arigatou gozaimasu.

Thank you for helping me.

Offers with 〜させていただきます

Humble way to offer to do something

ご説明させていただきます。

Go-setsumei sasete itadakimasu.

I will explain it to you. (humble expression)

Permission with 〜てもよろしいでしょうか

Very polite way to ask for permission

お手伝いさせていただいてもよろしいでしょうか。

Otetsudai sasete itadaite mo yoroshii deshou ka.

May I help you?

Special Usages and Considerations

Beyond the basic patterns, giving and receiving verbs in Japanese have several special usages and considerations that are important to understand for natural communication.

In-group vs. Out-group Considerations

The concept of "uchi" (in-group) and "soto" (out-group) is crucial for using giving and receiving verbs correctly. Your "in-group" typically includes yourself, your family, close friends, and your company or organization.

Key Rules

  • • When someone from your in-group gives something to someone outside, use あげる (or さしあげる)
  • • When someone from outside gives something to your in-group, use くれる (or くださる)
  • • When your in-group receives something from outside, use もらう (or いただく)

Examples of In-group Relationships

Family Example:

母は先生にプレゼントをあげました。

Haha wa sensei ni purezento o agemashita.

My mother gave a present to the teacher.

(Mother is in your in-group, teacher is out-group)

Company Example:

私の会社はお客様に商品を提供しています。

Watashi no kaisha wa okyaku-sama ni shouhin o teikyou shite imasu.

My company provides products to customers.

(Your company is in-group, customers are out-group)

Note: In-group and out-group boundaries can shift depending on the context. For example, when talking to someone outside your company about a colleague, your colleague is in your in-group. But when talking to your boss about the same colleague, they might be considered out-group.

Third-Person Relationships

When describing giving and receiving actions between two third parties (neither includes the speaker), the choice of verb depends on the speaker's perspective and relationship with the people involved.

When One Party is In-group

母は弟にお金をあげました。

Haha wa otouto ni okane o agemashita.

My mother gave money to my younger brother.

(Both mother and brother are in your in-group)

先生は母にアドバイスをくれました。

Sensei wa haha ni adobaisu o kuremashita.

The teacher gave advice to my mother.

(Mother is in-group, teacher is out-group)

When Both Parties are Out-group

田中さんは山田さんに本をあげました。

Tanaka-san wa Yamada-san ni hon o agemashita.

Mr. Tanaka gave a book to Mr. Yamada.

(Both are out-group, so あげる is used)

When both parties are outside your in-group, あげる is typically used regardless of their relationship to each other.

Giving and Receiving Favors

One of the most common uses of giving and receiving verbs in Japanese is to express doing favors for others or having others do favors for you.

〜てあげる

Doing something for someone else's benefit

子供に本を読んであげました。

Kodomo ni hon o yonde agemashita.

I read a book to the child (for their benefit).

〜てくれる

Someone doing something for your benefit

友達が宿題を手伝ってくれました。

Tomodachi ga shukudai o tetsudatte kuremashita.

My friend helped me with my homework.

〜てもらう

Having someone do something for you

髪を切ってもらいました。

Kami o kitte moraimashita.

I had my hair cut.

Cultural Note: In Japanese culture, explicitly stating that you did something for someone else's benefit (〜てあげる) can sometimes sound condescending. It's often better to use more neutral expressions unless you're talking about helping children or those who clearly need assistance.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Using あげる when くれる is appropriate

❌ 先生は私に本をあげました。

Sensei wa watashi ni hon o agemashita.

✓ 先生は私に本をくれました。

Sensei wa watashi ni hon o kuremashita.

When someone gives something to you or your in-group, use くれる, not あげる.

Mistake 2: Using the wrong particles

❌ 私は友達から本をあげました。

Watashi wa tomodachi kara hon o agemashita.

✓ 私は友達に本をあげました。

Watashi wa tomodachi ni hon o agemashita.

Use に to mark the recipient with あげる and くれる, and to mark the giver with もらう.

Mistake 3: Overusing 〜てあげる

❌ 社長に報告書を書いてあげました。

Shachou ni houkokusho o kaite agemashita.

✓ 社長に報告書を書きました。

Shachou ni houkokusho o kakimashita.

Using 〜てあげる when talking about doing something for a superior can sound presumptuous. Use a neutral expression or 〜ていただく if you want to be humble.

Mistake 4: Not considering social status

❌ 私は先生に本をあげました。

Watashi wa sensei ni hon o agemashita.

✓ 私は先生に本をさしあげました。

Watashi wa sensei ni hon o sashiagemashita.

When giving something to someone of higher status, use the humble form さしあげる instead of あげる.

Exercise 1: Choose the Correct Verb

Select the appropriate verb (あげる, くれる, or もらう) for each sentence.

1. 私は友達に誕生日プレゼントを＿＿＿＿＿。

Watashi wa tomodachi ni tanjoubi purezento o _______.

I gave a birthday present to my friend.

あげました (agemashita)

The speaker (I) is giving to someone else (friend), so あげる is used.

2. 先生は私に新しい本を＿＿＿＿＿。

Sensei wa watashi ni atarashii hon o _______.

The teacher gave me a new book.

くれました (kuremashita)

Someone else (teacher) is giving to the speaker (me), so くれる is used.

3. 私は母に花を＿＿＿＿＿。

Watashi wa haha ni hana o _______.

I gave flowers to my mother.

あげました (agemashita)

The speaker (I) is giving to someone else (mother), so あげる is used, even though mother is in the speaker's in-group.

4. 私は友達にケーキを＿＿＿＿＿。

Watashi wa tomodachi ni keeki o _______.

I received a cake from my friend.

もらいました (moraimashita)

The speaker (I) is receiving from someone else (friend), so もらう is used.

5. 母は弟におもちゃを＿＿＿＿＿。

Haha wa otouto ni omocha o _______.

My mother gave a toy to my younger brother.

あげました (agemashita)

Both mother and brother are in the speaker's in-group, but neither is the speaker, so あげる is used.

Summary

Key Takeaways

  • • Japanese has three main giving and receiving verbs: あげる (to give from speaker to others), くれる (to give from others to speaker), and もらう (to receive by speaker from others).
  • • The choice of verb depends on the direction of the action and the relationship between the giver and receiver.
  • • These verbs have honorific forms (さしあげる, くださる, いただく) used to show respect or humility.
  • • The concept of "in-group" vs. "out-group" is crucial for using these verbs correctly.
  • • These verbs can be combined with the て-form to express doing something for someone's benefit or having someone do something for you.
